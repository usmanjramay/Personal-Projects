You shared your first offer for a potential 4-apartment project on January 29, 2025 **(Appendix 1)** for €34,515 incl. VAT.

We explicitly rejected your offer on the same day, January 29, 2025 at 7:15 PM **(Appendix 2)**, in an email that states unambiguously:

"I can not agree to either of those two options, I understand you want to make more money than what we agreed contractually but I find this approach very unprofessional.

1. We still don't even know if 4 apartments are even possible [Evidence: the new contract and email you sent me this morning promising to lower the price if 4 apartments are in fact not possible] so it is clear that we have not even completed the analysis part of the original contract yet. This is also in line with what we clearly agreed verbally in all our meetings that there is no change of plan or contract till we have at least a directional okay from the commune, which we still do not have. [Evidence: your email to the commune after our meeting on monday and their response]."

This rejection was clear, explicit, and in writing. We also gave you clear direction not to do any work outside the original contract until we explicitly signed a new contract with you. This is evidenced by both the direction of the project and our communication going forward.

On March 18, 2025, you wrote to us via WhatsApp **(Appendix 3)**: *"You will also need to decide on the quotation for the four apartments project"* This is your own acknowledgment — nearly two months after our rejection — that the quotation for 4 apartments was still a future decision, not an existing agreement.

On April 1, 2025, you wrote via WhatsApp **(Appendix 4)**: *"4 apartment project you got the quote this we can discuss and find an agreement as I said last time."* Again, your own words: you still needed to *"find an agreement"* — because none existed.

You attempted to present yet another offer in June 2025, after we had already moved into the house, dated June 12, 2025 for €33,930 incl. VAT. The existence of multiple offers over months also proves no agreement existed.

**2. Our January 12, 2025 email was not authorization for any additional work outside the scope of our signed contract**

Our signed contract dated November 28, 2024 **(Appendix 5)** explicitly states that a detailed feasibility analysis for up to 5 apartments is part of the scope, in addition to full planning for up to 3 apartments.

Before addressing your misrepresentation of our January 12, 2025 email, we draw your attention to your own January 29, 2025 offer **(Appendix 1)**, which includes the statement: *"if the number of the apartment reduces for whatever reason a new offer will need to be made,"* along with the contingency clause:

> *"If during the development of the project the number of apartments is reduced for reasons of execution or regulation, a new joint agreement will have to be made in order to satisfy both parties."*

This is your own written acknowledgment that, as of January 29, 2025, you did not know whether 4 apartments were achievable. If even you — the licensed architect — did not know whether 4 apartments were feasible, then we were clearly still in the analysis phase. The analysis phase is covered by the original contract. You cannot bill us anything extra for work that falls within the scope of our signed contract.

In your demand letter, you cite our email of January 12, 2025 as authorization to perform work outside the scope of our signed contract because we wrote: "If 5 apartments are not possible with that, then we should stick to 4." This is a fundamental misrepresentation.  
The full context of our January 12, 2025 email thread **(Appendix 6)** shows that the entire exchange was about establishing the hard limits for the feasibility analysis — not authorizing design work.

**First,** we were rejecting your proposed approach outright. In the same email, we wrote: *"As we discussed previously, demolishing and rebuilding is not a viable option."* You had proposed demolishing part of the building in your January 11, 2025 proposal — we rejected that entirely. This was a fundamental disagreement about the basic premise of the project, not an agreement to proceed.

**Second,** we were questioning whether your idea even complied with commune regulations: *"I was under the impression that in order to get approval for leaving 'only 5m distance in the back' the rule was that the total depth of the building cannot exceed 10 meters?"* We were checking your work for basic regulatory compliance — something you, as the architect, should have verified before sending us a proposal.

**Third,** you responded on January 13, 2025 **(Appendix 6)** by confirming the uncertainty: *"We have to find a solution which is ok for you but as well which makes sense."* And critically: *"If we are doing a project for 4-5 apartments there are some points which can not be considered/kept of the left building."* In other words, you yourself were saying that 4-5 apartments might require demolition — the very thing we had just rejected. This exchange is clearly a pre-analysis discussion about constraints, not an agreement to proceed with a €33,930 project.

The statement "if 5 apartments are not possible, stick to 4" means exactly what it says: if, during your feasibility analysis (which was covered by the original contract), 5 apartments turn out not to be possible without demolition (which we had rejected), then analyze whether 4 apartments are possible within our stated constraints. This is a direction for the analysis, not authorization for you to perform €33,930 worth of design work and bill us retroactively.

Additionally, as we communicated explicitly from day one, any project was completely dependent on its feasibility both financially and timelines wise (given the hard deadline to vacate our previous rental).

In your January 29, 2025 email **(Appendix 1)**, you wrote: *"I can include a detailed estimation of the costs in my current offer of 29,500 Euro. This is a important basis for you to know the costs of the project."*

You yourself acknowledged that the cost estimation — which you called an *"important basis"* for decision-making — had not yet been provided. No reasonable client would authorize €33,930 in design work when the basic cost feasibility of the project had not yet been established. We asked you repeatedly for this cost estimate over the following months.

We also reject your suggestion that our continued engagement during this period constitutes implicit acceptance of a new contract. Our original signed contract **(Appendix 5)** covered a comprehensive scope: site measurements, feasibility analysis, full project design, floor plans, facades, building permit application, and submission to the commune. You were under a contractual obligation to deliver a complete plan for up to 3 appartments, and we were correspondingly available and responsive as part of that ongoing delivery. Continued communication during the performance of an existing contract cannot be construed as authorization of a separate, additional contract worth €33,930 for which no written agreement was ever signed, and no fee was ever agreed.

**4. Your conduct appears to violate professional ethics and consumer protection law**

As evidenced in Sections 1 and 2, no agreement existed for the work you claim to have performed; your offers were explicitly rejected in writing; you were told on multiple occasions not to perform any work outside the original contract; and no deliverables outside the scope of our signed contract were ever provided.
Additionally, as evidenced in Section 3, you were told explicitly, on multiple occasions, not to engage any services or companies on our behalf without a signed agreement.
Your conduct in connection with this demand therefore appears to engage provisions of both the professional ethics framework governing licensed architects (Ordre des Architectes et Ingénieurs — OAI) and the Code de la consommation luxembourgeois.

- **Manquement aux obligations professionnelles** and **manquement à la déontologie** under **Articles 8 and 9** of the Règlement grand-ducal du 17 juin 1992 déterminant la déontologie des architectes et des ingénieurs-conseils. Article 8 requires that *"pour toute mission, une convention doit être rédigée par écrit et signée par les deux parties"* (for every mission, a convention must be drawn up in writing and signed by both parties); no such signed convention exists for the alleged 4-apartment project. Article 9 further requires that any significant modification of the programme *"doit faire l'objet d'une convention additionnelle qui en mentionnera l'incidence financière"* (must be the subject of an additional written convention stating its financial impact); no such additional convention was ever signed.
- **Pratiques commerciales agressives** under Articles L.122-5, L.122-6 and L.122-7 of the Code de la consommation luxembourgeois — in particular **L.122-7(6)**, which treats demanding payment for services the consumer did not request as a per se aggressive practice: billing a client for services the client explicitly rejected and never authorized.

In addition, the invoices referenced in your demand (26.26.Q2, 27.26.Q2, 28.26.Q2) were never sent to us at any point prior to this demand letter. We have never seen them. Your letter stated: "Nous vous prions de bien procéder au paiement des factures ouvertes se trouvant en annexe au plus tard pour le 16.04.2026. À défaut de paiement, nous nous réservons le droit de vous mettre en demeure et d'engager une procédure de recouvrement, incluant, le cas échéant, des intérêts et frais judiciaires." (Please ensure that you pay the outstanding invoices listed in the attached document by April 16, 2026. Failure to pay will result in us issuing a formal notice and initiating debt recovery proceedings, which may include interest and legal fees.)

Demanding retroactive payment for unauthorized work with a 9-day deadline and threats of legal action appear to constitute pressure tactics that impair a consumer's freedom of choice and may constitute **pratiques commerciales agressives** under Articles L.122-5, L.122-6 and L.122-7 of the Code de la consommation luxembourgeois — and in particular **L.122-7(6)**, which treats as a per se aggressive practice any demand for payment for services the consumer did not request.

In your demand letter, you state that you submitted modified plans to the commune on our behalf without our knowledge or authorization, and after we explicitly told you on February 4, 2026 that no project is possible as we already live in the house with our two-year-old. You did so, as you admit in your own letter, specifically in connection with preparing this demand.

Your conduct in this regard appears to constitute:

- **Représentation sans pouvoir (acts beyond mandate)** under Article 1998 of the Code civil: acting on behalf of a client before a municipal authority without mandate or authorization. Under Article 1998, *"le mandant (…) n'est tenu de ce qui a pu être fait au-delà, qu'autant qu'il l'a ratifié expressément ou tacitement"* (the principal is bound by acts exceeding the mandate only insofar as they have ratified them expressly or tacitly). We granted no mandate for the commune submission and have not ratified it.
- A **dépassement de mission** and **manquement à la déontologie** under the Règlement grand-ducal du 17 juin 1992: a licensed architect may not represent a client before public authorities without explicit and current authorization to do so.

Furthermore, on January 13, 2025, you explicitly stated that *"you can not live inside during the construction works"* **(Appendix 6)**. This was correct professional advice at the time. However, as delays accumulated, you began — from March 2025 onwards — to try to convince us to proceed with full construction while living in the house **(Appendix 3)**. You wrote on March 18: *"We need to evaluate which parts of the house can be used and which areas should remain off-limits."* **(Appendix 3)** and when we asked how that would be feasible, you suggested a small space for us and our two-year-old to live in with massive construction works all around us **(Appendix 3)**.

This reversal of your professional advice coincided with your proposals for expanded scope, which represented significantly higher fees for your firm. Your conduct in this regard appears to constitute:

- **Manquement à la déontologie** under the Règlement grand-ducal du 17 juin 1992: a licensed architect must act in the client's interest and must not allow financial considerations to influence professional advice.
- **Pratiques commerciales trompeuses** under Articles L.122-2 and L.122-3 of the Code de la consommation luxembourgeois: misleading representations about project feasibility and timelines, which influenced our decision-making throughout the project.
- **Manquement à la diligence professionnelle** under Article L.122-1(1) of the Code de la consommation luxembourgeois, which prohibits commercial practices that are *"contraire[s] aux exigences de la diligence professionnelle"* and materially distort the consumer's economic behavior.

The sequence of events also speaks for itself: for approximately 8 months (June 2025 to February 2026), there was no communication between us. In our meeting on February 4, 2026, we told you very explicitly that no construction project was possible because we are now living in the house with our two-year-old. Shortly after that meeting, you submitted plans to the commune, created invoices we have never seen, and issued this demand with a 9-day payment deadline. We believe this to be a clear violation of ethical and professional standards.
