# Small Claims Court Response — Justice de Paix, Luxembourg

**Document Purpose:** Guide for responding to architect's €8,000 claim at small claims court  
**Court:** Justice de Paix (Magistrates' Court), Luxembourg  
**Claim Amount:** €8,000 (within €15,000 jurisdiction)  
**Status:** Court has issued summons; you must file response by deadline

---

## Part 1: Luxembourg Small Claims Court System Overview

### Jurisdiction & Limits

| Threshold | Jurisdiction |
|-----------|--------------|
| Up to €2,000 | Final decision (no appeal) |
| €2,000 – €15,000 | Appealable to District Court (Tribunal d'Arrondissement) |
| Your case: €8,000 | **Appealable** |

**Important:** Your €8,000 claim can be appealed if you lose, so this is not final.

**Source:** [Small Claims - European e-Justice Portal](https://e-justice.europa.eu/topics/money-monetary-claims/small-claims/lu_en)

### Procedure Type

The procedure before Justice de Paix is **oral**, meaning the court will hold a hearing where both sides present their case. However, written submissions (your written response) are critical to establishing your position before that hearing.

**Response Deadline:**
- Standard: 6-8 weeks from the summons date
- Under simplified proceedings (mise en état simplifiée): **3 months** to submit statement of defence (conclusions en réponse)
- **Check the court summons for the exact deadline in your case**

**Sources:** [Litigation & Dispute Resolution Luxembourg](https://www.iclg.com/practice-areas/litigation-and-dispute-resolution-laws-and-regulations/luxembourg) | [Time Limits - e-Justice](https://e-justice.europa.eu/topics/court-procedures/civil-cases/time-limits-procedures/lu_en)

### Cost Recovery

**Important Rule:** The losing side normally pays the court costs.

This means if you win, the architect pays your costs; if you lose, you pay the architect's costs. This incentivizes strong evidence.

## Part 2: Strategic Framework for Your Defence

### Your Core Argument (One-Sentence Summary)

> **The architect submitted an incomplete plan (exterior shell only, missing interior design) at the approval stage and is now demanding payment for a service that was never fully completed and cannot be used. The plan never satisfied the contractual requirement of being approvable as a habitable design, nor does it meet the contract's three-apartment minimum viability.**

### Key Legal Points to Argue

| Point | Explanation | Evidence |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Incomplete Performance** | Architect submitted only outer shell, omitting interior design required for approval | Plan documents showing missing interior details |
| **Non-Approval Status** | Plan was never formally approved; Payment Stage 3 (approval) was never reached | Court records, architect's communications |
| **Unusable Deliverable** | Plan cannot be used because you now live in the house; interior work is no longer feasible | Your occupancy, architect's own suggestions about splitting phases |
| **Breach of Scope** | Contract required design viable for minimum 2-apartment conversion; plan was incomplete for this | Original contract, plan assessment |
| **Unjust Enrichment** | Architect should not be paid for stage 3 (approval) when approval never occurred | Contract payment structure (stages 1, 2, 3) |
| **Contributory Delays** | Architect's delays contributed to inability to meet deadlines; not your fault | Email timeline showing delay requests from architect |

## Part 3: Evidence Organization for Court

### Critical Evidence List (Priority Order)

**Tier 1: MUST HAVE**
- [ ] Original signed contract (with payment stages clearly defined)
- [ ] Final plan submitted by architect (showing outer shell only, missing interior)
- [ ] Email chain showing architect suggested splitting phases ("outer shell now, interiors later")
- [ ] Communication showing you were living in house by time of dispute
- [ ] Court summons letter from architect

**Tier 2: STRONG SUPPORTING EVIDENCE**
- [ ] All correspondence with architect about project timeline and delays
- [ ] Proof of your tight deadline and rental apartment deadline
- [ ] Architect's acknowledgment that plan was incomplete (if any)
- [ ] Communication showing plan was never formally approved
- [ ] Any statement from architect about future interior modifications not requiring resubmission (evidence of his representations)

**Tier 3: CONTEXTUAL EVIDENCE**
- [ ] Communications about the 2-3-4-5 apartment feasibility scope (shows contract was limited in scope)
- [ ] Anything showing architect understood the urgency/deadline issue
- [ ] Project timeline showing when delays occurred

### How to Present Evidence

**Format:**
- Create a **document bundle/dossier** organized chronologically
- Label each document (Doc 1, Doc 2, etc.)
- Include a **document index** showing Doc #, Date, Type, Subject
- Include English translations if original is in French (Luxembourg courts often accept French but English translation helps)

**Copies:**
- Submit **certified copies** (not originals) to the court
- Keep originals for your records
- Note: Many courts require multiple copies — check with the court registry on how many copies of each document

**Emails:**
- Print as PDF from email client (preserves dates, sender, recipient)
- Include full email chains (shows conversation context)
- Include metadata (dates, time sent) clearly visible

## Part 4: Written Submission Structure (Key Points for Your Response)

### Format for Response to Court (Conclusions en Réponse)

**Language:** French is preferred but English may be accepted — check with court  
**Length:** 3-5 pages (concise, focused)  
**Structure:**

```
1. INTRODUCTION
   - Identify yourself as defendant
   - Summarize the claim (architect demanding €8,000)
   - Your position: CONTESTED

2. FACTUAL BACKGROUND (Chronological)
   - Original contract signed [date]: €8,000 for extension + 2-apt minimum design
   - Contract allowed 3+ apt feasibility study
   - [Dates]: Project delays from architect's requests
   - [Date]: You had to vacate rental apartment (hard deadline)
   - [Date]: Architect suggested submitting outer shell only
   - [Date]: Incomplete plan submitted (missing interior design)
   - [Date]: Plan never received formal approval from authorities
   - [Date]: You moved into house; project became unfeasible

3. LEGAL ARGUMENTS
   
   A. Payment Stage 3 Never Reached
      - Contract specified three payment stages: signing, submission, approval
      - Plan approval never occurred
      - Architect cannot demand Stage 3 payment when Stage 3 condition (approval) was not satisfied
      - This is a basic principle of contract law: payment is conditional on performance
   
   B. Performance Was Incomplete
      - Plan submitted was incomplete (exterior only, no interior design)
      - Architect himself suggested this phased approach
      - Plan cannot satisfy "approvable design" requirement of contract
      - Interior design is not a minor omission; it's essential to viability
   
   C. Deliverable is Unusable and Project Abandoned
      - Plan cannot be implemented (you now occupy the house)
      - Architect's delays contributed to missing the deadline
      - Project is now abandoned; no value from the plan
   
   D. Unjust Enrichment
      - If architect is paid for incomplete work, this is unjust enrichment
      - No benefit received (unusable plan)
      - No consideration given for full payment (no approval, incomplete design)

4. REQUEST FOR JUDGMENT
   - Demand court reject architect's claim
   - Request architect pay court costs
   - Request court order no payment due

5. ATTACHMENTS LIST
   - Document 1: Original Contract
   - Document 2: Plan Submission (exterior)
   - Document 3: Email chain re: phase splitting
   - [Etc. — list all attached evidence]
```

### Tone & Language Guidelines

**Do:**
- Be factual and specific (dates, amounts, contract references)
- Use calm, professional language
- Focus on contract terms and completion requirements
- Reference documents ("as shown in Document 3, dated...")
- Emphasize the architect's own suggestion about splitting phases

**Don't:**
- Use emotional language ("outrageous," "scam," "blackmail")
- Make personal attacks on the architect
- Speculate about intent ("he deliberately...")
- Overstate your case
- Make arguments you can't support with evidence

## Part 5: Key Arguments Summary (What Judge Needs to Hear)

### The Judge's Decision Will Hinge On

1. **What does the contract actually require?**
   - Your response: Design must be approvable as minimum 2-apartment conversion
   - Architect's likely argument: He provided what was asked

   **You win if:** Contract clearly states design must be complete enough to be approvable

2. **Was the plan approved?**
   - Your response: No — it was incomplete and never submitted for final approval
   - Architect's argument: Likely will claim he submitted it for approval

   **You win if:** You can show plan approval never occurred and architect acknowledged incompleteness

3. **Is payment conditional on approval?**
   - Your response: Yes — Stage 3 payment is for "plan approved"
   - Architect's argument: Likely will argue plan was "submitted" (Stage 2) so Stage 3 is due

   **You win if:** Contract language ties Stage 3 payment to actual approval (not just submission)

4. **Why is the plan unusable?**
   - Your response: Interior design is missing; you cannot construct interior while living in house
   - Architect's argument: Likely will say you can modify it later

   **You win if:** You explain clearly that Phase 2 cannot proceed without Phase 1 being built, and you cannot build Phase 1 while occupied

### The Winning Narrative

**If contract clearly says:**
- Stage 3 payment = upon plan approval
- Design must be viable for 2-apartment conversion
- Interior design is essential to viability

**Then you argue:**
- Plan was never approved (just submitted)
- Plan is not viable for 2-apartment conversion (missing interior)
- Therefore, Stage 3 conditions were not met
- Therefore, payment cannot be demanded

**Architect cannot collect Stage 3 if he failed to complete Stage 2.**

## Part 8: Success Factors

### What Works in Small Claims Cases

✅ **Strong evidence** — original contract, emails showing his suggestion to split phases, proof of incompleteness  
✅ **Clear chronology** — timeline showing delays, your deadline, when plan was submitted incomplete  
✅ **Contract-specific language** — reference exact contract clauses about payment stages and approval  
✅ **Practical explanation** — explain clearly why plan is unusable (interior design essential to 2-apt conversion)  
✅ **Professional tone** — judges favor composed, fact-based arguments over emotional ones  
✅ **Completeness** — address all three payment stages in your response, not just Stage 3  

### What Doesn't Work

❌ Emotion or character attacks  
❌ Vague references ("he was unprofessional")  
❌ Lack of supporting documents  
❌ Unclear timeline  
❌ Arguments that contradict your own evidence  
❌ Missing the court deadline
