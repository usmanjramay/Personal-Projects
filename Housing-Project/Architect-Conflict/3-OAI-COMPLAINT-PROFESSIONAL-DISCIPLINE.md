# OAI Complaint — Professional Discipline & Ethics Violation

**Document Purpose:** Guide for filing complaint with Luxembourg's Ordre des Architectes (OAI)  
**Authority:** OAI Discipline Commission & Discipline Council  
**Complaint Type:** Professional ethics violations, scope violations, unprofessional conduct  
**Focus:** Architect's duty of care, transparency, good faith, and professional conduct standards

---

## Part 1: The OAI (Ordre des Architectes et des Ingénieurs-Conseils)

### What the OAI Does

**Role:** Professional regulatory body for architects and consulting engineers in Luxembourg  
**Authority:** Investigates professional misconduct, enforces ethics standards, imposes discipline  
**Jurisdiction:** Over all architects licensed in/practicing in Luxembourg  
**Power:** Can issue warnings, cease-and-desist orders, fines, license suspension/revocation

**Sources:** [OAI Dispute Resolution](https://www.oai.lu/fr/15/accueil/pour-maitres-d-ouvrage/resoudre-un-litige/?CC=OK) | [OAI Legislation](https://www.oai.lu/files/Publications/Legislation/legisoai6.pdf)

### Professional Ethics Standards for Architects

Architects in Luxembourg must comply with:

| Standard | Your Case Violation |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Good Faith** | Architect must act honestly and transparently with clients |
| **Scope Compliance** | Architect must not perform work beyond contract without authorization |
| **Transparency** | Architect must disclose what work is being performed and its cost |
| **Duty of Care** | Architect must provide complete, professional deliverables |
| **Professional Dignity** | Architect must conduct disputes professionally, not through coercion |
| **Unjust Enrichment Prohibition** | Architect cannot charge for unauthorized work or incomplete performance |

### When OAI Discipline Is Applied

The OAI Discipline Council has authority over architects for:

1. **Violations of legal and regulatory prescriptions** concerning professional practice
2. **Acts contrary to professional delicacy and dignity** ← *Your case fits here*
3. **Violations of honor and probity** ← *Your case fits here*
4. **Professional ethics breaches** including:
   - Unauthorized scope expansion
   - Improper billing practices
   - Failure to deliver contracted work
   - Lack of transparency
   - Coercive tactics toward clients

**Source:** [OAI Legislation Document](https://www.oai.lu/files/Publications/Legislation/legisoai6.pdf)

### Submission Requirements

**Language:** French is standard; English may be accepted (confirm first)

**What to Include:**
1. Your identification (name, address, phone, email)
2. Architect's identification (full name, OAI license number if known, address)
3. Detailed description of professional misconduct
4. All supporting documents (contract, emails, invoices, plans)
5. Explanation of how professional standards were violated
6. Your signature and date

**Format:**
- Formal letter or structured complaint form
- Copies of all evidence (not originals)
- Clear organization and references to attached documents
- Professional, factual tone

## Part 3: Complaint Structure & Content Strategy

### The OAI Complaint Narrative

Unlike the DCP complaint (which focuses on "unfair commercial practices"), the OAI complaint focuses on **professional ethics violations**. The narrative is: "This architect violated fundamental professional standards of good faith, transparency, and scope compliance."

### Section 1: Complainant & Respondent Information

```

Date: [Today's Date]

COMPLAINANT (Client):
Name: [Your Full Name]
Address: [Your Full Address]
Email: [Your Email]
Phone: [Your Phone]
Date of Complaint: [Today]

RESPONDENT (Architect):
Name: [Architect's Full Name]

```

### Section 2: Professional Ethics Violation Details

This is the core of the OAI complaint. For each violation, you:
1. State the violation
2. Reference professional ethics standards
3. Provide specific evidence
4. Explain the harm

```
PROFESSIONAL ETHICS VIOLATIONS

Violation #1: UNAUTHORIZED SCOPE EXPANSION (€33,000 Demand)

Standard Violated: Architect's duty to obtain written authorization before expanding scope

Description:
The original contract clearly limited the scope to:
- House extension design (required)
- 2-apartment minimum viability design (required)  
- 3-apartment feasibility assessment ONLY (optional)
- 4-5 apartment feasibility assessment ONLY (optional, with separate fee if pursued)

The contract explicitly states that 4-5 apartment scenarios would incur additional fees 
only IF architect was directed to perform this work.

Professional Conduct Standard:
According to standard architectural practice, an architect must:
- Obtain written authorization before expanding scope
- Inform the client of any additional work planned
- Provide cost estimates before incurring expenses
- Not unilaterally perform costly work and demand retroactive payment

What Architect Did:
- Performed extensive "research" on 4-apartment and 5-apartment scenarios
- Never informed the client this work was being done
- Never provided written authorization from client
- Never provided cost estimates or timelines
- Never delivered completed feasibility studies or plans
- Demanded €33,000 payment retroactively, months after completion

Why This Violates Professional Ethics:
- Violates duty of transparency: client was not informed of the work
- Violates duty of good faith: architect performed speculative work, then billed for it
- Violates scope compliance: work was outside contracted scope
- Violates fiduciary duty: architect acted in own interest, not client's interest

Evidence:
- Document 1: Original Contract [shows limited scope for 4-5 apartment scenarios]
- Document 6: €33,000 Demand Letter [shows architect claims to have "worked on" scenarios]
- Document 2: Email chain [shows no authorization for additional work]
- Document 8: Project communications [shows no discussion of 4-5 apartment full plans]


Violation #2: LACK OF TRANSPARENCY (Both Invoices)

Standard Violated: Architect's duty to fully disclose work scope, deliverables, and costs

Description:
Professional architects must clearly communicate:
- What work will be performed
- What deliverables will be provided
- What the client will receive for payment
- Any changes to scope or cost

In this case:
- €8,000 Invoice: Architect submitted incomplete plan (exterior-only) and demanded full 
  Stage 3 payment without disclosing the plan was incomplete or unable to be approved
  
- €33,000 Invoice: Architect claimed to have performed "research" on scenarios without 
  explaining: What research? What deliverables? Why €33K? What is being charged for?

Why This Violates Professional Ethics:
- Violates transparency obligation: client not informed of incompleteness or status
- Violates disclosure obligation: no explanation of work allegedly performed for €33K
- Violates professional standard: clients should understand what they're paying for

Evidence:
- Document 3: Incomplete Plan [exterior-only, showing interior is missing]
- Document 4: Architect's own emails [mentioning "Phase 1: exterior, Phase 2: interior"]
- Document 5: Architect's response to completion question [acknowledges incompleteness]
- Document 6: €33,000 Demand [provides NO breakdown, no deliverables list, no justification]


Violation #3: BREACH OF GOOD FAITH OBLIGATION (Entire Engagement)

Standard Violated: Fundamental professional duty to act in client's interest, not against it

Description:
Professional ethics require architects to act honestly and in the client's best interest. 
This architect's conduct shows consistent breach:

Timeline of Bad Faith:
1. Architect suggested submitting incomplete plan "for speed" (knowing plan was incomplete)
2. Architect assured client that interior modifications would not require resubmission 
   (misrepresentation: interior design is essential to approvability)
3. Architect submitted incomplete plan and demanded full payment (knowing it couldn't be approved)
4. When client questioned €8,000 charge, architect escalated to court immediately 
   (coercive, rather than resolving concern)
5. Architect then demanded €33,000 for work client never authorized or received
6. Pattern: escalation to threats rather than professional resolution

Why This Violates Professional Ethics:
- Architects have fiduciary duty to clients: architect's actions show adversarial approach
- Architects must act with good faith: architect's pattern shows self-interest over client interest
- Architects must handle disputes professionally: immediate escalation to courts is unprofessional
- Architects must be truthful: architect misrepresented what "approval" meant, what "modifications" entailed

Evidence:
- Document 2: Email where architect suggested incomplete submission [shows architect knew it was incomplete]
- Document 4: Architect's follow-up email [confirming interior could be modified later without resubmission - 
  misrepresentation, as interior design is essential to approvability]
- Document 5: Court Summons [immediate escalation to legal action instead of professional resolution]
- Document 6: €33,000 Demand [demanding retroactive payment for unauthorized work]


Violation #4: UNJUST ENRICHMENT & IMPROPER BILLING (€8,000 Invoice)

Standard Violated: Architect's duty to provide complete, approvable deliverables for contracted payment

Description:
Contract structure: Three payment stages
- Stage 1: At signing (contract commitment)
- Stage 2: Upon plan submission (deliverable: complete plan)
- Stage 3: Upon plan approval (approval condition must be satisfied)

Architect's position: Demanding Stage 3 payment despite Stage 3 condition (approval) never being met.

Professional Standard:
Architects may only charge for completed work. Incomplete work = partial payment only, or no payment.

What Happened:
- Architect submitted incomplete plan (exterior-only, missing interior design)
- Plan is not approvable because it lacks essential interior design
- Architect cannot claim approval stage payment when no approval has occurred
- This is improper billing for incomplete performance

Why This Violates Professional Ethics:
- Violates completion obligation: deliverable is incomplete
- Violates billing ethics: cannot bill for stage not reached (approval stage not reached)
- Violates professional standards: incomplete architecture = no right to full payment
- Violation of unjust enrichment principle: architect should not be enriched for incomplete work

Evidence:
- Document 1: Contract [shows Stage 3 payment tied to "upon plan approval"]
- Document 3: Plan submitted [exterior only, objectively incomplete]
- Document 7: Communication showing plan never approved [no approval from authorities]
- Document 10: Professional standards on approvable design [interior is essential to 2-apartment viability]


Violation #5: UNPROFESSIONAL CONDUCT IN DISPUTE RESOLUTION (Both Invoices)

Standard Violated: Architect's duty to handle client disputes professionally and honestly

Description:
When a client questions billing, professional conduct requires:
- Listening to the concern
- Explaining the work performed
- Resolving the matter professionally (discussion, negotiation, mediation)
- As last resort only: legal action

This architect's conduct:
- €8,000 disputed? Immediately escalated to court (no attempt at resolution)
- €33,000 demanded? Threatened collection action (no offer to discuss or mediate)

Why This Violates Professional Ethics:
- Violates professional dignity standard: coercive approach rather than professional dialogue
- Violates "honor and probity": threatening conduct reflects poorly on profession
- Violates good faith: refusing to discuss concerns before escalating to courts
- Demonstrates unprofessional attitude toward clients: treating as adversaries, not partners

Evidence:
- Document 5: Court Summons [showing immediate escalation, no prior negotiation attempt]
- Document 6: €33,000 Demand Letter [threatening language, no offer to discuss]
- Document 8: Email chain [showing no good-faith dialogue about concerns]
- Timeline [showing rapid escalation from question to legal action]
```

## Part 4: Best Practices for Maximum Impact

### What Makes OAI Complaints Successful

✅ **Direct reference to professional standards** — Show that architect violated OAI ethics rules, not just general principles  
✅ **Specific, dated violations** — "January 15 email showed..." beats "architect was unprofessional"  
✅ **Evidence of pattern** — Don't just cite one incident; show systematic breach  
✅ **Professional tone** — OAI respects calm, detailed complaints more than emotional ones  
✅ **Clear harm** — Explain concrete impact (€33K demand, incomplete deliverable, project failure)  
✅ **Documentation** — Every claim backed by attached evidence  
✅ **Distinction from contract law** — Focus on ethics violations, not just contract disputes (that's for court)  
✅ **Comparison to standards** — Show how architect's conduct deviates from what other architects do  

### What Doesn't Work

❌ **Emotional language** ("outrageous," "scammer," "blackmail")  
❌ **Personal attacks** ("he's dishonest," "he's greedy")  
❌ **Vague complaints** ("he didn't act professionally")  
❌ **Contract disputes treated as ethics violations** — OAI is for ethics, not contract interpretation  
❌ **Missing evidence** — OAI won't investigate without documentation  
❌ **Rambling narrative** — Be concise and structured

## Part 5: OAI Complaint Process Timeline

### How OAI Handles Complaints

| Phase | Timeline | What Happens |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| **Reception** | 1 week | OAI secretariat confirms receipt, assigns case number |
| **Initial Assessment** | 2-3 weeks | Director + Commission member reviews complaint for validity |
| **Competence Analysis** | 1-2 weeks | OAI confirms architect is member in good standing |
| **Complaint Forwarded** | 1 week | Complaint is sent to architect for written response |
| **Architect Response** | 4 weeks | Architect submits written response to allegations |
| **Commission Review** | 4-6 weeks | Commission reviews complaint and architect response |
| **Amicable Settlement Attempt** | 2-4 weeks | Commission often facilitates dialogue between parties |
| **Settlement or Escalation** | Variable | If settled: case closed. If not: may escalate to Discipline Council |
| **Discipline Council** | 2-3 months | Formal hearing if serious violations alleged |
| **Decision & Notification** | 2-4 weeks | OAI notifies both parties of findings |

**Total Timeline:** 4-6 months typical (can be faster if settled early, slower if escalated)

### Possible Outcomes

**Best Case:**
- OAI Commission finds violations confirmed
- Architect agrees to withdraw €33,000 demand and reduce €8,000 charge
- Formal reprimand issued to architect's file
- OAI issues public statement about incident (warning to profession)

**Likely Case:**
- OAI Commission confirms ethics violations
- Issues reprimand or warning
- Recommends architect refrain from further threats
- May recommend architect participate in ethics mediation
- €33,000 demand referred back to court (not OAI jurisdiction) but with official finding of misconduct

**Conservative Case:**
- OAI acknowledges complaint
- Finds some violations substantiated
- Issues warning to architect to improve conduct
- Creates official record on architect's file

**Minimum Benefit:**
- Official disciplinary record created
- Can be referenced in court proceedings as evidence of professional misconduct
- Creates ongoing liability for architect if he continues problematic behavior

### Why OAI Matters Even If It Doesn't Resolve Disputes

OAI complaints are valuable not because they resolve money disputes, but because they:
1. **Create official record** of professional misconduct on architect's permanent file
2. **Influence court proceedings** — judges consider professional discipline findings
3. **Create reputational damage** — other potential clients will see disciplinary history
4. **Deter future misconduct** — architect knows his conduct is being scrutinized
5. **Protect other clients** — disciplinary findings may prevent architect from repeating pattern

## Part 9: Key Differences: OAI vs. Court vs. DCP

### How These Three Processes Are Different

| Aspect | Court (Justice de Paix) | DCP (Consumer Protection) | OAI (Professional Discipline) |
|--------|------------------------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Focus** | Contract interpretation & payment obligation | Consumer protection law violations | Professional ethics standards |
| **Question Asked** | "Is the €8,000 owed under contract?" | "Are these unfair commercial practices?" | "Did architect violate professional ethics?" |
| **Jurisdiction** | Money disputes up to €15,000 | Consumer fraud/unfair practices | Professional conduct |
| **Decision** | Court judgment (binding) | DCP findings (advisory) | OAI discipline (binding for members) |
| **Process** | Formal trial with hearing | Investigation with findings | Investigation with possible hearing |
| **Timeline** | 4-8 months | 2-4 months | 4-6 months |
| **Outcome** | Money judgment (who pays what) | Enforcement action if violations found | Discipline (warning, reprimand, cease-and-desist) |
| **Appeal** | Yes, to District Court | Limited | Yes, to higher OAI bodies |

**Why File All Three:**
- Court: Resolves €8,000 payment dispute
- DCP: Documents unfair commercial practices, triggers consumer protection enforcement
- OAI: Documents professional misconduct, creates disciplinary record, influences court findings
