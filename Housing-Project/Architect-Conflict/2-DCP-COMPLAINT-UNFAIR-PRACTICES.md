# Directorate for Consumer Protection (DCP) Complaint — Unfair Commercial Practices

**Document Purpose:** Guide for filing complaint with Luxembourg's Directorate for Consumer Protection  
**Authority:** Direction de la protection des consommateurs (DPC), Ministry of Economy  
**Complaint Type:** Unfair commercial practices (misleading claims, predatory billing, threats)  
**Case:** €33,000 unjustified demand + €8,000 court proceedings + threatening language

---

## Part 1: The Directorate for Consumer Protection (DCP)

### What the DCP Does

**Role:** Government enforcement agency that investigates unfair commercial practices by businesses  
**Authority:** Can investigate, compel evidence, issue findings, and refer cases for prosecution  
**Scope:** All consumer complaints involving unfair practices in service contracts  
**Enforcement:** Can result in fines, cease-and-desist orders, public warnings, and ban of practices

**Sources:** [DCP Legal Protection](https://mpc.gouvernement.lu/en/le-ministerie/protjur.html) | [Reporting Unfair Practices](https://guichet.public.lu/en/citoyens/citoyennete/protection-consommateur/pratiques-deloyales/denonciation-pratique-deloyale.html)

### Types of Unfair Practices (Your Case)

The DCP investigates "unfair commercial practices," which include:

| Practice Type | Your Case Example |
|---------------|-------------------|
| **Misleading Claims** | Architect claiming he "did work on" 4-5 apt studies when no full plans were contracted |
| **Undisclosed Costs** | Demanding €33,000 for work not authorized, without prior agreement |
| **Coercive Tactics** | Threatening court action and collection if you don't pay fabricated charges |
| **Scope Violation** | Performing work beyond contracted scope, then billing for it |
| **Unjust Enrichment** | Charging for services never requested or authorized |
| **Deceptive Practices** | Misrepresenting what "work on a feasibility study" means (study vs. full plans) |

### Submission Requirements

**Language:** French preferred (English may be accepted, but French is official)

**What to Include:**
1. Comprehensive description of the unfair practice
2. All supporting documents (original contract, invoices, emails, letters)
3. Your personal information (name, address, contact details)
4. Date of complaint submission
5. Your signature

- Written complaint (letter or form)
- Copies of all documents (not originals)
- Clear, logical organization

## Part 3: Complaint Structure & Content Strategy

### Complaint Narrative Framework

Your complaint should tell a clear story: "Professional performed unauthorized work, concealed its unauthorized nature, and now demands payment through threats."

### Section 1: Identification

```
COMPLAINT TO THE DIRECTORATE FOR CONSUMER PROTECTION

Date: [Today's Date]
Language: [French or English]

COMPLAINANT:
Name: [Your Name]
Address: [Your Address]
Email: [Your Email]
Phone: [Your Phone]

RESPONDENT (ARCHITECT):
Name: [Architect's Full Name]
Professional License #: [If known]
Business Address: [Architect's Address]
Email: [Architect's Email]
Professional Registration: OAI Member [Reference if available]

SUBJECT: Unfair Commercial Practices — Unjustified Billing for Unauthorized Scope of Work
```

### Section 2: Factual Chronology (Be Specific & Detailed)

Present facts in strict chronological order with dates. This section is your evidence narrative.

```
FACTUAL BACKGROUND:

2024 [Month]:
- We engaged Architect X for house renovation extension
- Signed contract for €8,000 clearly defining scope:
  * Extension design (required)
  * 2-apartment minimum viable design (required)
  * Feasibility assessment for 3 apartments (optional)
  * Feasibility assessment for 4-5 apartments (optional, additional fee if pursued)

2024 [Month] - [Month]:
- Project experienced multiple delays from architect's requests
- We communicated tight deadline due to rental apartment vacation deadline
- Architect advised speeding process

[Date]:
- Architect proposed submitting only exterior shell (Phase 1) for approval
- Claimed interior modifications could be made later without resubmission
- We agreed to this phased approach to meet deadline

[Date]:
- Architect submitted incomplete plan (exterior shell only, missing interior design)
- Plan does not include interior layout needed for 2-apartment conversion viability
- Plan is incomplete and cannot be approved for habitation without interior design

[Date]:
- Our rental deadline arrived; we moved into the house
- Project could no longer proceed (cannot renovate interior while occupied)
- Project abandoned

[Date]:
- Architect sent invoice for €8,000 (Stage 3 payment) despite:
  * Plan never approved (approval conditions not met)
  * Plan incomplete (interior design missing)
  * Project abandoned due to architect's delays and incomplete deliverable

[Date]:
- When we challenged the €8,000 invoice, architect escalated to court proceedings

[Date]:
- Court issued summons regarding €8,000 claim

[Date]:
- Architect sent SECOND demand letter for €33,000 additional payment
- Claims included "research" on 4-apartment and 5-apartment scenarios
- NO contract for full plans of these scenarios
- NO authorization given for this work
- NO deliverables provided (no completed feasibility studies, no plans)
- Demands payment without basis, threatening court action and collection procedures
- Claims work was "done at his own initiative" but now demands compensation

CURRENT STATUS:
- €8,000 court case pending
- €33,000 demand letter threatening collection action
```

**Best Practice Note:** The more specific and dated, the better. DCP investigators use these dates to cross-reference documents you attach.

### Section 3: Identification of Unfair Practices

For each unfair practice, cite the specific behavior and explain why it violates consumer protection law.

```
UNFAIR COMMERCIAL PRACTICES IDENTIFIED:

1. SCOPE VIOLATION & UNJUST ENRICHMENT (€33,000 Demand)

   Practice: Architect claims to have "worked on" 4-apartment and 5-apartment feasibility studies 
   without authorization and now demands €33,000 payment.

   Why Unfair:
   - Original contract explicitly limited feasibility studies to "assessment" (optional)
   - Contract did NOT authorize full plans for 4-5 apartment scenarios
   - No written agreement authorizing €33,000 in extra work
   - Architect never informed us he was doing this work or that we would be billed
   - Architect performed work speculatively and now demands retroactive payment
   - This violates principle of good faith: no contract, no authorization, no disclosure

   Legal Violation: Unfair commercial practice under Luxembourg Consumer Code:
   - Misleading claim (implying work was authorized when it wasn't)
   - Undisclosed obligations (demanding payment for work never contracted)
   - Unjust enrichment (attempting to extract payment for unauthorized services)


2. COERCIVE & THREATENING TACTICS (Both Invoices)

   Practice: Architect uses court proceedings and collection threats to pressure payment
   for services not properly completed or authorized.

   Why Unfair:
   - €8,000 invoice: Court proceedings for incomplete, unapproved plan
   - €33,000 demand: Threatening "collection procedures" for fabricated charges
   - Threatens legal action for payment owed only if charges are actually valid
   - Pattern shows escalation: when questioned, immediately courts/threatens instead of 
     addressing legitimate concerns

   Legal Violation: Coercive commercial practices
   - Threatening legal action to intimidate payment for questionable charges


3. MISLEADING INFORMATION ABOUT DELIVERABLES (€8,000 Invoice)

   Practice: Architect presented incomplete plan as fulfilling contractual obligations
   and now demands Stage 3 (approval) payment despite plan never being approved.

   Why Unfair:
   - Contract clearly ties Stage 3 payment to "plan approved"
   - Architect submitted incomplete plan (exterior-only, missing interior)
   - Incomplete plan cannot be approved for habitation
   - Architect himself suggested phasing, knowing interior was incomplete
   - Now claims payment is due despite approval condition never being satisfied
   - This is misleading claim that work was "completed" when it was incomplete

   Legal Violation: Misleading commercial practice
   - Misrepresenting completion of contractual obligations
   - Demanding payment despite conditions not being met


4. LACK OF TRANSPARENCY (€33,000 Demand)

   Practice: Architect performs costly work without prior notification, authorization,
   or agreement to pay, then bills retroactively.

   Why Unfair:
   - Standard professional practice requires written authorization before incurring costs
   - Architect never informed us of extensive "research" on 4-5 apartments
   - Architect never provided scope, timeline, or cost estimate for this work
   - Architect never delivered the promised feasibility studies
   - Payment demand appeared with no documentation, no work samples, no justification

   Legal Violation: Violation of transparency obligations
   - Failure to disclose work scope, costs, timeline before incurring charges
   - Failure to provide documentation of work allegedly performed
```

### Section 5: Specific Legal Violations

```
LEGAL VIOLATIONS IDENTIFIED:

This case involves multiple violations of Luxembourg's Consumer Code (Code de la Consommation):

1. Unfair Commercial Practice (Pratique Commerciale Déloyale)
   - Unauthorized scope expansion
   - Unjustified billing for unsolicited work
   - Lack of transparency about costs and authorization

2. Misleading Information
   - Presenting incomplete work as meeting contractual obligations
   - Claiming "feasibility studies were performed" without delivering them
   - Demanding payment without disclosure of basis or scope

3. Coercive Tactics
   - Threatening court action for disputed charges
   - Threatening collection procedures for fabricated claims
   - Escalation pattern: when questioned, immediately escalates to legal action

4. Violation of Good Faith Principle
   - Professional obligation to inform customer before incurring costs
   - Obligation to obtain authorization before expanding scope
   - Obligation to deliver work or explain non-performance
```

```

We formally request that the Directorate for Consumer Protection:

2. DETERMINE whether the €33,000 demand violates unfair commercial practices law

3. DETERMINE whether the €8,000 invoice is valid given incomplete performance

4. ISSUE FINDINGS regarding professional misconduct and unfair practices

5. RECOMMEND to the architect that:
   - €33,000 demand be withdrawn immediately
   - €8,000 invoice be reduced to reflect partial/incomplete performance
   - Architect refrain from further legal action and collection threats

6. Consider whether this case warrants referral for prosecution under consumer protection law

7. Issue public guidance or warning about similar practices (for consumer protection)
```

## Part 4: Best Practices for Maximum Impact

### What Makes DCP Complaints Successful

✅ **Clear, chronological narrative** — DCP investigators are busy; tell the story simply  
✅ **Specific dates** — "January 15, 2024" beats "sometime in January"  
✅ **Direct evidence** — Attach original documents, not summaries  
✅ **Explicit violations** — Reference Luxembourg Consumer Code articles (provides legal framework)  
✅ **Clear harm** — Explain exactly how you were harmed (€33K demand on fabricated charges)  
✅ **Professional tone** — Emotional language undermines credibility with investigators  
✅ **Completeness** — Don't force DCP to chase missing documents; provide everything upfront  

### What Doesn't Work

❌ **Vague complaints** ("architect was unprofessional")  
❌ **Emotional language** ("outrageous," "scammer," "blackmail")  
❌ **Missing documents** — DCP won't investigate without evidence  
❌ **Rambling narrative** — DCP investigators read dozens of complaints; be concise  
❌ **Character attacks** — Focus on behavior/practices, not personality  
❌ **Unsupported claims** — Every violation must be backed by document evidence

## Part 5: Strategic Timing & Cross-Filing

### When to File DCP Complaint

**Recommended:** File DCP complaint **after** sending your response letter to the architect.

**Why?**
1. You want architect to know complaint is coming (letter gives notice)
2. Shows you've exhausted good-faith resolution attempts (strengthens complaint)
3. Coordinated timing: court response + warning letter + formal complaint = comprehensive pressure

**Timeline Suggestion:**
- Week 1: Respond to court summons (€8,000 case)
- Week 2: Send formal response letter to architect (threatens complaints)
- Week 3: File DCP complaint + OAI complaint (formal complaints go in)

### Coordination with Other Complaints

Your three-pronged approach (court, DCP, OAI) creates:

1. **Legal pressure** (court case) — financial risk to architect
2. **Consumer protection pressure** (DCP) — enforcement risk, reputation risk
3. **Professional discipline pressure** (OAI) — license/reputation risk

Architect faces three separate investigations if he pursues €33K demand, making it expensive and risky for him.

## Part 6: What Happens After You File

### DCP Investigation Process

| Phase | Timeline | What Happens |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| **Intake** | 1 week | DCP confirms receipt, assigns case number |
| **Initial Review** | 2-3 weeks | DCP staff reviews complaint, determines if it merits investigation |
| **Investigation** | 4-8 weeks | DCP contacts architect, requests documents, seeks explanation |
| **Findings** | 2-4 weeks | DCP prepares written findings and recommendations |
| **Notification** | 1 week | DCP notifies you of findings and any actions taken |
| **Follow-Up** | Ongoing | If violations found, DCP may issue cease-and-desist, fine, or refer for prosecution |

**Total Timeline:** 2-4 months typical (can vary based on complexity)

### Possible Outcomes

**Best Case:**
- DCP investigates, finds violations
- Issues cease-and-desist order to architect
- Recommends charges be withdrawn
- Creates official record that practices were unfair

**Likely Case:**
- DCP investigates both invoices
- Finds €33,000 demand lacks basis
- Recommends €8,000 be resolved through court process (not DCP jurisdiction)
- Creates record that architect's practices were problematic

**Conservative Case:**
- DCP acknowledges complaint
- Concludes matter is primarily contractual (belongs in court)
- But still creates official record of complaint and allegations

**Minimum Benefit:**
- Official record that you reported unfair practices
- Can be referenced in court case as evidence of predatory pattern
- Creates liability risk for architect if he continues threatening behavior

## Part 9: Sample Complaint Opening (Adapt This)

```
DIRECTORATE FOR CONSUMER PROTECTION

RE: Complaint for Unfair Commercial Practices — Unjustified Billing and Coercive Tactics
    by [Architect Name], OAI Member, [Address]

Dear Sir or Madam:

I am writing to file a formal complaint regarding unfair commercial practices by an architect 
with whom I engaged for a house renovation project.

In [Month 2024], I signed a contract for €8,000 to design a house extension and assess 
feasibility for multi-unit conversion. The contract explicitly defined scope and payment stages.

The architect then performed work beyond this scope — claiming to have conducted extensive 
research on 4- and 5-apartment scenarios — without authorization, and is now demanding €33,000 
additional payment for this unauthorized work.

Additionally, when I questioned his initial €8,000 invoice (for incomplete work), he escalated 
to court proceedings rather than addressing the legitimate concern.

This pattern constitutes unfair commercial practice: unauthorized scope expansion, lack of 
transparency, unjustified billing, and coercive escalation.

I attach complete documentation and formally request investigation.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
```
