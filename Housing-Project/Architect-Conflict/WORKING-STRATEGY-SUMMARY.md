## DISPUTE 1: €33,930 DEMAND — ARCHITECT'S CLAIMS vs. OUR COUNTERARGUMENTS

### Claim 1: "Client authorized 4-apartment work" (citing Jan 12 email)

**Architect says:** Your email of Jan 12 saying "If 5 apartments are not possible with that, then we should stick to 4" is authorization.

**Our counterarguments (strengthened per user feedback):**

1. **Conditional feasibility preference, not authorization.** This is analysis-phase language. The original contract already covered feasibility analysis for 2-5 apartments.

2. **Same email chain shows FUNDAMENTAL DISAGREEMENT, not agreement.** In the same Jan 11-13 exchange:
   - Architect proposes demolishing part of the building
   - Client explicitly rejects demolition: "demolishing and rebuilding is not an option unfortunately"
   - Architect admits 4-5 apartments may not be possible WITHOUT demolition: "there are some points which can not be considered/keept of the left building"
   - Client questions whether the architect's plan even complies with commune regulations: "I was under the impression that in order to get approval for leaving 'only 5m distance in the back' the rule was that the total depth of the building cannot exceed 10 meters?"
   
   **Key argument:** How can there be an "agreement" when the client is rejecting the architect's fundamental approach (demolition) and questioning whether the architect has even read the regulations? The email chain shows a clear disagreement, not agreement.

3. **Architect himself doesn't know if 4 apartments are possible without demolition.** His Jan 13 response: "We have to find a solution which is ok for you but as well which makes sense." This is exploration, not execution.

4. **The plan the architect shared on Jan 11 was fundamentally flawed.** He hadn't read the regulations. Client caught errors the architect should have known. This is the analysis phase — checking feasibility — not authorized design work.

**Evidence files:** EMAIL-JAN-11-INITIAL-PROJECT-SUBMISSION.md, EMAIL-JAN-29-OFFER-WITH-CONTINGENCY.md

### Claim 2: "Work was mutually agreed" (single-family plan designed for 4 apartments)

**Architect says:** Both parties agreed to submit a single-family plan that meets 4-apartment requirements.

**Our counterarguments:**

1. **Client followed architect's professional guidance based on a false premise.** Architect said changes could "easily" be made after approval ("later on we will add" — Feb 18 email). This turned out to be FALSE.

2. **Commune confirmed IN WRITING that the approved plan cannot be modified.** The only options are: (a) pay for permit as-is, or (b) formally withdraw and reapply. This means the architect's advice was wrong — the "we'll modify it later" strategy doesn't work.

3. **The "agreement" was based on architect's misrepresentation.** An agreement based on false professional advice is not a valid agreement. The architect should have known the commune's rules.

4. **User's context for asking the commune:** User asked about REDUCING the project scope because of delays and rental deadline pressure — caused by the architect's delays.

**Evidence:** Feb 18 email, Commune's written response

### Claim 3: "No signed contract doesn't matter" (case law argument)

**Architect says:** Luxembourg case law allows payment even without a signed contract if architect acted "at client's request."

**Our counterarguments (revised per user feedback):**

1. **USER FEEDBACK: The architect's phrase "no agreement could be reached" in his demand letter may NOT be as strong as initially thought.** The architect is framing it as: "I did the work at client's request, then client refused to pay, so no agreement on payment could be reached." We should NOT rely heavily on this phrase as it could be interpreted in his favor.

2. **Much stronger evidence of no agreement:**
   - **Multiple offers prove no agreement.** Architect made offers on Jan 27, Jan 29, and June 12. If agreement existed, why keep making new offers? Each new offer proves the previous one was rejected.
   - **Jan 29 email:** Architect writes "when the agreement is there, otherwise we are turning around" — proving he knew no agreement existed at that point.
   - **WhatsApp messages:** Client repeatedly tells architect "I can't commit to anything until you make me a good offer." Client told architect IN PERSON the offer was not acceptable.
   - **March/April WhatsApp:** Architect says "we can talk about the offer for 4 apartments" — still negotiating months later.
   - **Jan 29 email same day:** Client explicitly writes "I can not agree to either of those two options...I am not willing to pay any extra payment...I find this approach very unprofessional"
   - **Email evidence of explicit rejection:** Client has email saying explicitly that work outside the contract is not authorized.

3. **"At client's request" requires actual request, not conditional feasibility questions.** A conditional statement ("if 5 not possible, stick to 4") during analysis phase is not a "request" for €33k of design work.

**Evidence:** Multiple offer emails, WhatsApp messages, Jan 29 rejection email

### Claim 4: "Initial contract only covered analysis, not 4-apartment development"

**Architect says:** Original contract didn't cover 4-apartment development, so additional work was needed.

**Our counterarguments (strengthened per user feedback):**

1. **The distinction between "analysis" and "development" is the architect's invention.** As a client, how is Usman supposed to know the difference? The contract says the architect will analyze feasibility for 2-5 apartments. From the client's perspective, figuring out if 4 apartments are possible IS the analysis.

2. **Architect's own Jan 29 offer includes contingency clause:** "if the number of apartments reduces for whatever reason a new offer will need to be made." This is the architect's OWN ADMISSION that as of Jan 29, he doesn't know if 4 apartments are possible. If even the architect doesn't know, then we are STILL in the analysis phase.

3. **The original contract explicitly says:** "The analysis of the demand for a 5-apartment building will be done in this request and a new offer is to be issued for a project of this scale." This means: analyze first (included in contract), THEN if feasible, issue new offer for actual design work. The architect never completed the analysis conclusively before demanding payment for "development."

4. **Cost estimation was still pending.** Architect's own Jan 29 email: "I can include a detailed estimation of the costs...This is an important basis for you to know the costs of the project." Without cost estimation, how can there be a decision to proceed?

**Evidence:** Original contract, Jan 29 offer with contingency, Jan 29 email about cost estimation

## LUXEMBOURG LEGAL FRAMEWORK (from research)

### Relevant Law
- **Code civil Art. 1108:** Contract requires consent, capacity, object, lawful cause
- **Code civil Art. 1134:** Contracts binding in good faith
- **Code civil Art. 1147:** Damages for non-performance
- **Code civil Art. 1168:** Conditional obligations
- **Code de la consommation:** Unfair commercial practices
- **Law of 13 Dec 1989:** Architect professional organization (OAI)
- **Grand-Ducal Regulation 17 June 1992:** Architect professional ethics

### Court Procedure
- Justice de Paix: jurisdiction up to €15,000
- Oral procedure, no lawyer required
- Objection within 30 days of notification
- Filing is free of charge

### DCP Filing
- Via MyGuichet.lu or by post
- Must attempt direct resolution first (demand letter response serves this purpose)
- 3-month processing time
- Fines up to €120,000 or 4% of turnover

### OAI Filing
- Written complaint to oai@oai.lu
- Disciplinary Council investigates
- Can impose professional sanctions
