## PART ONE: INTRODUCTION AND OVERVIEW

**1.** On 23 March 2026, the Justice de Paix de Luxembourg issued conditional payment order L-OPA2-4005/26 at the request of ATAM Architecture & Management SARL ("ATAM" or "the Architect"), ordering the Opposant, Usman Javaid Ramay ("the Client"), to pay the sum of €2,193.75 plus statutory interest, representing the third installment under a contract for architectural services dated 28 November 2024. (Appendix M)

**2.** The Client respectfully submits that the third installment is not owed. The payment trigger for the third installment — obtaining the building permit — was met only in the most formal and technical sense. The permit obtained is, in substance, unusable: it covers only an exterior shell that was never completed to the agreed scope, cannot be modified to reflect what the Client actually needs, and was obtained through a process marred by significant professional failures on the Architect's part. A permit that the Client cannot act upon is not the deliverable contemplated by the contract.

**3.** Beyond the specific question of the third installment, the Client submits that the Architect's conduct throughout this engagement was so fundamentally deficient — in terms of competence, communication, professional advice, and fidelity to the agreed mission — that the contract should be pronounced resolved (*résolu*) for non-performance under Article 1184 of the Code civil luxembourgeois. The consequence of resolution is restitution: the Architect should be required to return the €6,581.25 already paid by the Client, who received nothing of practical value in return.

**4.** In the alternative or in addition, the Client claims damages under Article 1147 Code civil for losses directly caused by the Architect's failures, including the surprise building permit tax of €14,932 which the Architect never warned the Client about and which the Client was left to absorb without any professional guidance.

**5.** This opposition sets out the facts in chronological order (Part Two), followed by the legal arguments (Part Three), and concludes with the prayer for relief (Part Four). Evidence is referenced by Appendix letter throughout.

## PART TWO: STATEMENT OF FACTS

### Section 2.1 — The Contract and the Client's Circumstances

**6.** On 28 November 2024, the Client entered into a written contract with ATAM for architectural services relating to a residential property (Appendix A). The agreed scope included:

- Extension of the garden level, ground floor level, and addition of one floor;
- The plan was to "contain the possibility and will be organised in such a way as to later make a vertical cadastre for a building with 2 official apartments";
- An optional third apartment analysis depending on regulations;
- A separate analysis of a potential 5-apartment project, with any such expanded scope to be covered by a new and separate offer;
- Full professional services through to building permit, including site analysis, as-built plans, feasibility studies, preliminary draft, project design, building permit application, floor plans, facades, site plan, 3D images, and OAI architect's certificate.

**7.** The agreed contract price was €8,775 incl. VAT (€7,500 + 17% VAT), payable in three installments:

- 1st installment (50%): €4,387.50 — upon signing and project start;
- 2nd installment (25%): €2,193.75 — upon submission to the municipality;
- 3rd installment (25%): €2,193.75 — upon obtaining the building permit.

**8.** The contract stated a possible project start date of December 2024.

**9.** From the very outset, the Client communicated to the Architect a hard and non-negotiable timeline constraint: the Client's family was subject to a court-mandated deadline to vacate their previous rental apartment. The project was therefore entirely conditional on feasibility within that timeline. The Architect confirmed that the timeline was achievable. It was this professional confirmation — by a licensed architect who had reviewed the Client's situation — that induced the Client to sign the contract and commit to the project. Had the Architect said otherwise, the Client would not have proceeded.

### Section 2.2 — Delayed and Defective Start (December 2024 – January 2025)

**10.** Despite a contract start date of December 2024, the Architect did not share any initial analysis until mid-January 2025 — more than a month late. (Appendix C)

**11.** When the analysis finally arrived, it was fundamentally flawed in two distinct respects:

(a) It proposed demolishing part of the existing building. The Client had explicitly informed the Architect that demolition was not financially or logistically feasible for the family. This was not a minor design preference but a stated constraint that any compliant design had to respect.

(b) The proposed design appeared to violate the commune's building-depth regulations (the "10-metre rule"). This is a basic element of communal planning law that a licensed architect preparing a building permit application for that commune was professionally obliged to verify before producing any plans.

**12.** Critically, it was the Client — not the Architect — who identified both of these errors, having taken it upon himself to read the relevant communal regulations. (Appendix C)

**13.** Between 14 and 27 January 2025, there was a sustained thread of communications between the Client and the commune to clarify the 10-metre exception and staircase setback rules. Throughout this period, the record shows the Architect was relying on the Client to research communal requirements rather than discharging this as a basic professional function. This inverted the proper professional relationship and consumed weeks of the already-constrained timeline.

### Section 2.3 — Rejected Offer for Expanded Scope (January 2025)

**14.** On 29 January 2025, the Architect sent the Client an offer for a separate 4-apartment project at €34,515 incl. VAT. (Appendix D) The offer contained a contingency clause in the Architect's own words: *"If during the development of the project the number of apartments is reduced for reasons of execution or regulation, a new joint agreement will have to be made in order to satisfy both parties."*

**15.** The Client explicitly rejected this offer on the same day, in writing, at 7:15 PM: *"I can not agree to either of those two options."* (Appendix E)

**16.** The Client's written rejection of the expanded-scope offer is significant. From that point forward, the Architect was working exclusively under the original contract. There was no basis to proceed as if a larger engagement had been agreed.

### Section 2.4 — Continued Design Work and Professional Misrepresentations (February 2025)

**17.** On 5 February 2025, the Architect shared an outer shell plan with a note that *"The plan can work for an apartment building of 3–4 apartments without problems later on"* — confirming that even at this late stage, the apartment feasibility had not been determined, despite this being a core element of the contracted scope.

**18.** On 7 February 2025, the Architect sent the Client an explicit professional recommendation: *"I advise that we don't lose time and submit the project asap. Interior changes we can do as well later."* This was an unqualified professional representation that it was appropriate to submit the plans in their current state, and that interior modifications could be made after the permit was granted.

**19.** On 18 February 2025, the Architect sent a further update stating — in his own words: *"Some new interior walls we removed so it gets approved for the extension later on we will add."* (Appendix H) This was the Architect's own unilateral decision. The Client had not requested the removal of interior walls. The Architect made this change based solely on his own judgment and his representation that the walls could be "added later."

**20.** These representations from 7 February and 18 February 2025 were material to the Client's decision to proceed with the submission. Both the Client and his wife were present when these assurances were given in person. The Client relied on the Architect's professional judgment that a permit could be obtained on the basis of the exterior shell alone, with interior modifications to follow. No licensed architect who understood the commune's procedures would have given this assurance if it was incorrect.

### Section 2.5 — Submission Errors and Commune Rejection (February–March 2025)

**21.** On 26 February 2025, the Architect asked the Client to sign an authorisation for submission to the commune. The Client declined and submitted the plan directly himself.

**22.** On 12 March 2025, the commune raised formal objections to the submitted plan. One objection concerned balconies that were not permitted in that zone. The Architect expressly conceded that this was a careless error on his part. (Appendices B-8 and B-9)

**23.** This error — including a design feature that contravened zoning rules — is precisely the kind of basic regulatory check that a licensed architect managing a building permit application is professionally required to conduct before submission. Its inclusion caused further delay, further communications with the commune between 12 and 17 March 2025, and consumed more of the Client's already-compressed timeline.

**24.** The final exterior shell plan was generated on 20 March 2025. The Client himself submitted the final plan and energy assessment to the commune on 27 March 2025.

### Section 2.6 — Architect Conditions Future Services and Takes Unauthorised Actions (March–April 2025)

**25.** On 28 March 2025, the Architect sent the Client a WhatsApp message stating, in his own words: *"After the permit I can not invest more time in your project after you get the building permit, if we don't agree to work together for the whole project. This should be clear soon."*

**26.** This message is significant. The Architect was explicitly conditioning the completion of his contractual obligations on the Client agreeing to a larger and more lucrative engagement. This is not professional conduct consistent with a licensed architect's duties to his client.

**27.** On 1 April 2025, the Architect arranged a visit to the Client's property by a construction company — without any authorisation from the Client. When the Client asked why, the Architect responded: *"spent time with people for your project not related with building permit."* (Appendix B-2) The Client had explicitly told the Architect not to take any actions outside the original contract without prior agreement. The Architect ignored this instruction.

**28.** In the same 1 April 2025 exchange, the Client stated: *"with how delayed things are and now everyone saying the plan is not feasible, I am not even sure if we can do the project in time."* The Architect dismissed this concern. (Appendix B-2)

**29.** On 23 April 2025, the Architect sent emails to soil analysis companies regarding the Client's property — again without any authorisation from the Client, and again after the Client had explicitly instructed the Architect not to take any actions without agreed authorisation. (Appendix B-10)

### Section 2.7 — The Architect's Reversal on Living During Construction

**30.** On 13 January 2025, the Architect gave the Client the following professional advice: *"you can not live inside during the construction works."* (Appendix B-1) This was consistent with what any person would expect of a construction project of this scale — and it shaped the Client's entire understanding of the project's logistics.

**32.** This reversal coincided directly with the Architect's push for expanded scope engagements representing significantly higher fees. The Client did not accept this position. It was not a professionally reasonable position, and it directly contradicted the Architect's own earlier advice.

### Section 2.8 — Building Permit Granted; Architect Non-Responsive to Critical Questions (May–June 2025)

**33.** The building permit was granted by the commune on or around 8 May 2025. The permit covered the exterior shell plan as submitted.

**34.** After the permit was issued, the Client urgently needed from the Architect: (a) a realistic cost estimate for construction; (b) information regarding the building permit tax of €14,932 which the Client had received entirely without prior warning; and (c) an assessment of what minimal viable renovation could realistically be completed given the timeline constraints.

The Architect — as the licensed professional managing the building permit application — was the appropriate person to have advised the Client about this cost in advance, or at the very least to have been prepared to advise on it when it arose. When the Client raised the issue, the Architect seemed surprised and promised to look into it. He never did. (Appendices B-5, B-6)

**38.** On 12 June 2025, the Architect sent new commercial offers: a 4-apartment plan for €33,930 and project management services for €53,820. At this point, the Client had already made the decision to move into the property. These offers came weeks too late to be of any practical use.

**39.** Faced with the Architect's non-responsiveness, the approaching rental deadline, the absence of any feasible plan, the uncertainty about construction costs, and the Architect's explicit statement that he would not continue working on the project unless the Client signed up for a larger engagement, the Client made the only available decision: the family moved into the property. **41.** In July 2025, the Architect sent another reminder. The Client was already living in the house and painting it. No further substantive communication occurred between approximately July 2025 and late January 2026 — a period of approximately seven months.

**42.** On 4 February 2026, the Architect requested an in-person meeting. The Client attended. **44.** This written confirmation from the commune directly contradicts the Architect's explicit and repeated professional assurance — given both in person (with both the Client and his wife present) and in writing on 18 February 2025 (Appendix H) — that modifications and reductions could be made after approval.

**45.** On 9 March 2026, ATAM sent the Client a payment reminder for the third installment. (Appendix N)

**46.** On 10 April 2026, the Client emailed the Architect to note that the square footage analysis promised in writing in June 2025 and again at the February 2026 meeting had still not been delivered, and to request help simplifying the plan to something the family could actually use before any discussion of final payment. **49.** If the Architect genuinely designed the project for "the maximum of the apartments" rather than for the agreed purpose — the Client's family home with an option for future conversion to two apartments — then he acted outside the mandate conferred by the contract. Further, and in any event, the permit actually obtained is for a single-family house exterior shell, not for "maximum apartments." The Architect's April 10 statement therefore confirms, rather than refutes, the Client's position: the delivered outcome fulfils neither the purpose agreed by the parties, nor the purpose the Architect now claims to have been pursuing.

This condition exists because the Architect's delays and the unusable building permit made any meaningful renovation impossible before the rental deadline forced the family to move in. The building permit as issued covers only the exterior shell of a proposed extension; it cannot be modified; the interior was never designed; and there is no feasible path to executing the permit for the purpose for which it was obtained.

## PART THREE: LEGAL ARGUMENTS

### Section 3.1 — The Third Installment Is Not Owed: The Contractual Trigger Was Not Met in Substance

**51.** The contract provides that the third and final installment of €2,193.75 is due "upon obtaining the building permit." ATAM relies on the formal issuance of the permit on or around 8 May 2025 as the trigger for this payment.

**52.** The Client submits that the payment trigger must be interpreted in accordance with Article 1156 of the Code civil luxembourgeois, which provides that contracts are to be interpreted according to the common intention of the parties, not the literal meaning of words. The common intention of the parties was that the Architect would obtain a building permit that fulfilled the contracted purpose — a permit for a project that the Client could actually implement. A permit obtained for a plan that:

- was submitted without the interior modifications the Architect himself had identified as necessary, in reliance on his own representation that they could be completed later;
- cannot be modified to reflect the Client's actual needs, contrary to the Architect's explicit professional assurance that modifications could be made after approval;
- was designed (on the Architect's own account in his April 10 email) for a purpose other than the agreed mission;
- cannot be acted upon by the Client in any feasible way,

is not the permit contemplated by the contract. Form without substance does not satisfy a contractual obligation.

**53.** Separately and additionally, the principle of *exception d'inexécution* (*exceptio non adimpleti contractus*) — consistently applied in Luxembourg jurisprudence as a general principle deriving from the synallagmatic nature of contracts — provides that a party may withhold performance of its own obligation while the other party is in ongoing and material non-performance. As set out in Section 3.2 below, the Architect's non-performance was systematic and material. The Client is entitled to withhold the third installment on this basis independently of the question of resolution.

### Section 3.2 — Resolution of the Contract for Non-Performance (Article 1184 Code civil)

**54.** Article 1184 of the Code civil luxembourgeois provides that the resolutory condition is always implied in synallagmatic contracts where one party fails to perform. The aggrieved party may either compel performance or seek judicial resolution with damages. Resolution operates with retroactive effect (*ex tunc*), requiring restitution of what has been paid. Luxembourg case law applies this principle consistently in architect-fee disputes (Cour de cassation, 23 May 2019, 87/19; 27 April 2017, 39/17).

**55.** The Client submits that ATAM's non-performance was material across multiple dimensions.

**a) Failure to conduct basic regulatory verification before producing plans**

The Architect's first analysis, delivered over a month late, proposed demolition (explicitly ruled out by the Client) and appeared to violate the commune's 10-metre building-depth rule. Both errors were identified not by the Architect but by the Client from his own reading of communal regulations. (Appendix C) A licensed architect managing a building permit application for a property in that commune is professionally required to verify these requirements before producing plans. This failure wasted weeks of the Client's constrained timeline and required extensive corrective communications with the commune.

**b) Submission of a plan with regulatory errors — the balcony mistake**

The Architect submitted (or advised submission of) a plan that included balconies not permitted in the relevant zoning area. The commune rejected the plan on this basis. The Architect conceded this was, in his own words, "just a careless mistake." (Appendices B-8, B-9) This is a basic professional failure that caused further delay and corrective work.

**c) Incorrect professional advice about permit modifiability**

The Architect explicitly advised the Client, in writing on 18 February 2025, that interior walls had been removed but could be "added later on." (Appendix H) He explicitly advised on 7 February 2025 that the Client should submit without completing the interior design and that "interior changes we can do as well later." These were professional representations, not casual suggestions, made to both the Client and his wife. The commune has confirmed in writing that the permit cannot be modified — the only options are to proceed with the full approved plan or to withdraw and reapply from scratch. (Appendix L) The Architect was wrong. A licensed architect who understands the relevant commune's procedures should have known this before advising the Client to proceed on this basis.

**d) Failure to advise about the building permit tax**

At no point during the engagement did the Architect advise the Client about the building permit tax of €14,932. This tax was directly connected to the permit application the Architect was managing. The Client received the tax notice without any prior warning or professional guidance. When the Client raised the issue, the Architect had no immediate answer and promised to look into it. He never did. (Appendices B-5, B-6)

**e) Systematic non-responsiveness during the critical post-permit period**

Between 6 May and 11 June 2025, the Client sent multiple unanswered reminders requesting urgently needed information: construction cost estimates, guidance on the permit tax, and an assessment of viable options. (Appendices B-5, B-6, B-7) This non-responsiveness, during the period when the Client most needed professional guidance to make time-sensitive decisions, directly caused the Client to act without professional input. The decision to move into the property — which ended the practical possibility of executing the original project — was made in the vacuum created by the Architect's silence.

**f) Failure to deliver promised deliverables**

The contract provided for a comprehensive scope of services. The Architect never delivered: the apartment feasibility analysis (which remained unresolved even as of 5 February 2025); the square footage analysis promised in writing in June 2025 and reiterated in February 2026 (Appendix O); construction cost estimates despite repeated requests; or any professionally adequate assessment of how the Client's timeline constraint could be managed.

**g) Actions outside the mandate without authorisation**

The Architect arranged a visit by a construction company to the Client's property without authorisation (Appendix B-2) and contacted soil analysis companies regarding the Client's property without authorisation (Appendix B-10), after the Client had explicitly instructed him not to take any actions outside the original contract without prior agreement.

**56.** Taken individually, several of these failures would be material. Taken together, they represent a systematic and comprehensive failure to perform the contracted mission. The Client paid €6,581.25 — 75% of the total contract price — and received, in return, an exterior-shell building permit for a project he cannot execute, obtained through a process marred by the Architect's own errors and misrepresentations. This is not a case of a minor shortfall in performance. The deliverable is not merely imperfect — it is unusable.

**57.** The permit is unusable for five independent reasons:

(a) It was submitted without interior modifications the Architect had identified as necessary, based on his own representation that these could be completed after approval;

(b) It cannot be modified after approval, contrary to the Architect's explicit professional assurance that was a material reason for the Client's decision to proceed with the submission in its current form;

(c) It was obtained through a process in which the Architect's own delays — beginning with the late, flawed first analysis in January 2025 and compounded by the balcony error in March 2025 — made the originally intended project impossible to execute within the Client's stated timeline;

(d) It covers design elements (including the basement and balconies) that the Architect later acknowledged as too costly, structurally problematic, or impermissible in the relevant zone; and

(e) The Architect himself stated on 10 April 2026 that the project was "never for single family use but for the maximum of the apartments" — a purpose that contradicts the agreed contract and which is not reflected in the permit obtained in any event.

**58.** Resolution under Article 1184 requires that ATAM return the sums already paid by the Client. The Client paid €4,387.50 (first installment) and €2,193.75 (second installment), totalling €6,581.25. Restitution of this sum is sought.

**59.** The doctrine of *prestations inexploitables* supports this conclusion. Where professional services result in deliverables that are genuinely unusable for their intended purpose, fees paid for those services are not retained. The Client acknowledges that Article 1315 Code civil places the burden of proof on him. That burden is met here by the evidence — much of it in the Architect's own written words — demonstrating the fundamental inadequacy of what was delivered.

**60.** Similarly, the principle of *mission non accomplie* — applied in Luxembourg jurisprudence to architect-fee disputes — holds that fees are tied to phases actually completed to a useful standard. The OAI standard architect contract (Article 24) provides that on termination, an architect is entitled to fees for services actually rendered plus 30% for services not yet rendered. In this case, what was rendered was not merely incomplete — it was counterproductive, resulting in a permit the Client cannot use. No fee is justified on this basis.

### Section 3.3 — Damages for Defective Performance (Article 1147 Code civil)

**61.** In the alternative or in addition to resolution, Article 1147 Code civil provides for damages flowing from non-performance or defective performance of a contractual obligation. No external, unforeseeable, or irresistible circumstances (*force majeure*) excuse the Architect's conduct.

**62.** The Client's losses attributable to the Architect's failures include but are not limited to:

(a) The building permit tax of €14,932, which the Client was required to pay without any prior warning or professional guidance. Had the Architect fulfilled his professional obligation to advise about foreseeable costs associated with the building permit application, the Client would have been able to factor this into his decision-making.

(b) The costs of time and effort expended by the Client in personally identifying regulatory errors, researching communal regulations, communicating with the commune, correcting the Architect's mistakes, and substituting for the professional services he had paid for but was not receiving.

(c) The ongoing costs and living conditions imposed on the Client's family — including the absence of a window in the main living area — directly caused by the Architect's delays and the unusable permit making any meaningful renovation impossible before the rental deadline.

(d) Such further consequential losses as the Client may establish at hearing.

### Section 3.4 — Acts Beyond the Mandate (Article 1998 Code civil)

**63.** Article 1998 of the Code civil provides that the principal is bound by acts performed within the mandate; acts exceeding the mandate do not bind the principal absent express or tacit ratification.

**64.** The Architect's own statement on 10 April 2026 — that the project was "never for a single family use but for the maximum of the apartments" — if taken at face value, means the Architect was proceeding as if the mandate were something other than what the contract said it was. The contract was for the Client's family home, with an option for future conversion to two apartments and a separate (rejected) offer for an expanded multi-apartment project. At no point did the Client mandate the Architect to design for "the maximum of the apartments."

**65.** The Client did not ratify any expanded mandate — he explicitly rejected the only formal offer for expanded scope on 29 January 2025 (Appendix E) and repeatedly raised the timeline constraint and the need for a feasible single-family solution throughout the engagement.

**66.** A contractor who executes beyond the agreed scope of work cannot claim payment for the excess. Where the excess work — designed for a purpose the Client never agreed to — has rendered the permit useless for the Client's actual purpose, no payment can be justified on any basis.

### Section 3.5 — Misleading Professional Representations (Code de la Consommation)

**67.** The Architect's conduct also engages the Code de la consommation luxembourgeois.

**68.** Article L.122-2 prohibits misleading commercial practices involving false information or deceptive representations about the extent of a professional's commitments. The Architect's representation that the building permit could be modified after approval — made explicitly in writing (Appendix H) and in person — was either factually incorrect or deceptively incomplete about the nature of the services being rendered. The commune has confirmed in writing this representation was wrong. (Appendix L) A licensed architect, whose professional training and daily work involves building permit procedures, should have known the applicable rules of the relevant commune.

**69.** Article L.122-3 prohibits misleading omissions — failure to provide material information a consumer needs to make an informed commercial decision. The Architect's failure to inform the Client about the building permit tax of €14,932 constitutes a misleading omission. This was material information directly connected to the permit application the Architect was managing. Had the Client known, it would have been a relevant factor in deciding how to proceed.

**70.** Article L.122-1(1) prohibits unfair commercial practices contrary to professional diligence. The pattern of conduct described throughout this opposition — systematic delays, regulatory errors, misrepresentations about permit modifiability, conditioning of continued services on expanded commercial engagement, unsolicited actions on the Client's property — is contrary to the standard of professional diligence expected of a licensed architect practising in Luxembourg.

### Section 3.6 — Deontological Obligations of a Licensed Architect (Règlement grand-ducal du 17 juin 1992)

**71.** The Règlement grand-ducal du 17 juin 1992 governing OAI deontology imposes specific professional obligations on licensed architects, which are relevant to this Court's assessment of the Architect's conduct and entitlement to fees.

**72.** Article 8 requires that every mission be the subject of a signed written convention between architect and client. The original contract satisfies this requirement. However, when the Architect began pursuing an expanded scope involving additional apartments, there was no written convention signed by the Client for any expanded engagement. The only formal offer for expanded scope was explicitly rejected in writing (Appendix E).

**73.** Article 9 requires that any significant modification of the programme be the subject of an additional written convention stating the financial impact. The Architect's unilateral removal of interior walls (Appendix H) was a significant modification of the scope of works to be submitted. It was made without any written convention and without any prior discussion of the financial or procedural implications with the Client.

**74.** The general deontological obligation requires an architect to act in the client's interest, to apply communal planning procedures correctly, and not to allow financial considerations to distort professional advice. The Architect's conduct — advising submission of an incomplete plan, conditioning future services on expanded engagement, reversing advice about habitability during construction in the context of a higher-fee proposal — is inconsistent with these obligations.

### Section 3.7 — Gestion d'Affaires (Article 1375 Code civil)
