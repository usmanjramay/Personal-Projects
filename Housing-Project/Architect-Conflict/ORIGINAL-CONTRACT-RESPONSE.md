>
> **Primary legal basis — Restitution of the €6,581.25:**
>
> - **Article 1184 Code civil** (résolution for non-performance): the primary anchor. In a synallagmatic contract, the aggrieved party may seek judicial *résolution* of the contract with retroactive restitutive effect. Luxembourg Cour de cassation has applied this doctrine consistently in architect-fee disputes (e.g., 23 May 2019, 87/19; 27 April 2017, 39/17). See `LEGAL-ARTICLES-REFERENCE.md` for verbatim text.
>
> **Supporting doctrines (jurisprudential):**
>
> - **Mission non accomplie** — architect-specific Luxembourg jurisprudence: fees must correspond to phases actually completed; where the mission is not accomplished (design errors, regulatory misses, unmodifiable permit, no feasible timeline → no usable deliverable), fees are reduced or refused. OAI standard contract Art. 24 supports phased-fee entitlement.
> - **Prestations inexploitables** — where the client can prove deliverables are genuinely unusable for their intended purpose, non-payment or restitution is justified. **Burden of proof on us** (Art. 1315 Code civil).
> - **Exception d'inexécution** — defensive doctrine; we were entitled to suspend further payment given the architect's ongoing non-performance.
>
> **Supporting Code civil articles:**
>
> - **Article 1147 Code civil** (responsabilité contractuelle): damages for non-performance or defective performance of contract. Basis for consequential damages claim.
> - **Article 1156 Code civil** (contract interpretation — common intention of parties): counters any narrow literal reading of the November 2024 contract. The common intention was a feasible, usable family-home permit within our timeline.
> - **Article 1375 Code civil** (gestion d'affaires — limits on reimbursement): *"le maître dont l'affaire a été bien administrée…"* — any reimbursement depends on whether the work was well managed. Design errors, delays, regulatory misses, and an unmodifiable permit fail that test.

## 5. We believe your actions have caused our family harm
We told you very clearly from the begining about the hard deadline tied to our rental situation and our need to move in to the house latest by June 2025. You told us explicitly that the project could be completed in that timeline, without this confirmation from a professional we would not have pursued the project at all. In addition to already being late, that analysis was fundamentally flawed *(Appendix C)* as it proposed demolishing part of the building (which we had explicitly told you was not feasible for us financially or timelines wise), and it appeared to violate the commune's building depth regulations. I had to point out these errors myself based on my reading of the regulations. These are basic regulatory requirements that we believe you, as the licensed architect, should have verified before working on anything let alone sharing it. Such misses caused unneeccasary delays, including the rejection from the commune of the initial submission due to blaconies being planned which were not permitted. As evidenced by **(Appendix B-8)** you seem to agree that this was just a careless mistake, which prompted the question from me "why did we put it there in the first place then?" **(Appendix B-9)**.

These unneccsary delays made the project timeline completely unrealistic. As a licensed professional, you should have advised us clearly and early if the timeline was not realistic. Instead, you continued to pursue expanded scope (more apartments, project management at €53,820) while the basic deliverables remained unfulfilled. We raised our concerns regarding the timelines feasibility with you multiple times during the project while you kept telling us not to worry even up to 1st of April as evidenced by your response to me stating "with how delayed things are and now everyone saying the plan is not feasible, l am not even sure if we can do the project in time.:(" to which you replied "Everythink is feasibly just depending on the budget:) According to this for the exectution we can adapt the project plans. For commune this is not important. We will discuss" **(Appendix B-2)**

Furthermore, on January 13, 2025, you explicitly stated that *"you can not live inside during the construction works"* **(Appendix C)**. This was correct professional advice at the time. However, as delays accumulated, you began — from March 2025 onwards — to try to convince us to proceed with full construction while living in the house **(Appendix B-1)**. You wrote on March 18: *"We need to evaluate which parts of the house can be used and which areas should remain off-limits."* **(Appendix B-1)** and when we asked how do you think that would be feasible you suggested a tiny space for us and our two year old to live in with massive construction works all around us. **(Appendix B-1)**

This reversal of your professional advice coincided with your proposals for expanded scope, which represented significantly higher fees for your firm. Your conduct in this regard appears to constitute:
- **Manquement à la déontologie** under the Règlement grand-ducal du 17 juin 1992: a licensed architect must act in the client's interest and must not allow financial considerations to influence professional advice.
- **Pratiques commerciales trompeuses** under Articles L.122-2 and L.122-3 of the Code de la consommation luxembourgeois: misleading representations about project feasibility and timelines, which influenced our decision-making throughout the project.
- **Manquement à la diligence professionnelle** under Article L.122-1(1) of the Code de la consommation luxembourgeois, which prohibits commercial practices contrary to the requirements of professional diligence that materially distort the economic behaviour of the average consumer.

Additionally you never once mentioned that there are taxes attached to the building permit itself. We were surprised to recieve a bill of 14,932 euros allong with the permit. When we pointed this out to you, you also seemed surprised by the charges and promised to look into it **appendix** which you never did despite multiple reminders.
Infact between April, 2025 and mid June, 2025, we sent you multiple reminders for critical information points we needed from you *appendix xx and yy* inclduing "Hi Almir, is everything okay? Its been two weeeks, can you please just let me know if you are sharing some data otherwise I would like to go ahead and ask the commune already without it?! 🙏". Since you were not responding and we were running out of time, it becaeme very clear that an extension project was no longer feasible. We therefore decided during this time to move into the house and informe the commune that we would like to modify the permit.

The square footage analysis you had promised both in writing in June 2025 and again at our in-person meeting in February 2026 has never been provided. When we raised this again in April 2026 — specifically asking for your help to reduce the plan to a version we could actually use — you did not engage with this outstanding obligation. On March 9, 2026, you had already sent a payment reminder for the third installment. When we followed up in April 2026 to request the analysis before any final payment discussion, your response was not to provide the promised analysis but to assert, for the first time, that the project had never been intended for our family's use at all *(see below)*.

You told us to submit only the outer shell of the building with maximum possible extensions for approval and do the actual plan and feasibility checks later, in order to speed up the permit. We followed your guidance becasue you explicitly assured us — including in a meeting with both myself and my wife — that reductions and modifications to the approved plan could easily be made after approval. You reinforced this in writing on February 18, 2025 **(Appendix H)**: *"Some new interior walls we removed so it get approved for the extension later on we will add."* You confirmed the same logic applied to the basement when we asked on April 23, 2025 whether it could be removed from the plan if we were not building it — you did not raise any concern about the permit being unmodifiable **(Appendix B-10)**.

This assurance was the basis on which we agreed to proceed with the permit application for the exterior shell alone. We relied on your professional representation that modifications specially reductions could easily be done afterwards.

The commune has since confirmed **in writing** **(Appendix L)** that the approved permit cannot be modified. Our only options are to pay for the permit as issued or to formally withdraw the application and reapply from scratch. Your professional advice that changes could be made *"later on"* was it seems incorrect. As a licensed architect, you should have known the commune's procedures. This along with the delays mentioned earlier has left us with a building permit that is entirely unusable for our intended purpose.

Your conduct in this regard appears to constitute:
- **manquement à la déontologie** and **manquement aux obligations professionnelles** under the Règlement grand-ducal du 17 juin 1992: a licensed architect is obligated to provide accurate professional advice and to know the applicable municipal planning procedures.
- **Pratiques commerciales trompeuses** under Articles L.122-2 and L.122-3 of the Code de la consommation luxembourgeois: you provided incorrect professional advice about permit modifiability, which influenced our decision to proceed with the permit application and to rely on your representation that the scope could be reduced later.

Your email of April 10, 2026 raises a further issue that bears directly on both the third payment and the adequacy of the deliverable. Responding to our request for help in simplifying the plan to a usable version, you stated: *"The configuration of the project was never for a single family use but for the maximum of the apartments"* and *"The project goal was never to have it as one family house with custom made wishes."*

These statements are irreconcilable with the common intention of the parties. Under Article 1156 of the Code civil, contracts are interpreted according to the common intention of the parties rather than the literal meaning of their terms. Our common intention, communicated to you explicitly from the outset, was a feasible and usable plan for our family home — entered into under a hard deadline driven by our rental situation. Apartment configurations were included in the contract as options to be analyzed as a possible future investment, not as a purpose that would override our family's immediate practical needs.

If — as you now claim — you oriented your work from the outset toward "maximum apartments" rather than our agreed purpose, you acted outside the scope of your mandate. Under Article 1998 of the Code civil, acts performed beyond the mandate are not binding on the principal who has not ratified them expressly or tacitly. We have not ratified any reorientation of the project toward apartment maximization. Furthermore, even on your own account, the permit actually obtained — for a single-family house exterior shell — falls entirely short of your stated objective of "maximum apartments." Your April 10 statement therefore confirms, rather than refutes, our position: the delivered outcome fulfils neither the purpose we agreed nor, on your own telling, the purpose you claim to have been pursuing.
