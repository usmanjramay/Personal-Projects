## 1. Code civil luxembourgeois

Source: [legilux.public.lu — Code civil (consolidated)](https://legilux.public.lu/eli/etat/leg/code/civil/20250420)

### Article 1156 — Contract interpretation (common intention of parties)

**Subject:** Rule of contract interpretation — the judge must seek the common intention of the parties rather than stop at the literal meaning of the words. Luxembourg has NOT adopted the 2016 French contract-law reform, so Art. 1156 retains its original 1804 text.

**Used in:** `ORIGINAL-CONTRACT-RESPONSE.md` (as reference for potential small-claims-court argument; the architect cannot insist on a narrow literal reading of the original contract if the common intention was a usable feasible family home).

**NOT applicable to:** "représentation sans pouvoir" — despite being used this way in earlier drafts, this article has nothing to do with representation. For unauthorised representation see Article 1998 below.

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. 1156. On doit dans les conventions rechercher quelle a été la commune intention des parties contractantes, plutôt que de s'arrêter au sens littéral des termes.

**English translation:**

> Art. 1156. In agreements, one must seek what was the common intention of the contracting parties, rather than stopping at the literal meaning of the words.

### Article 1372 — Gestion d'affaires (principle)

**Subject:** When a person voluntarily manages another's affairs, the gestor tacitly undertakes to continue and complete the management, with all accessory duties, as if they had received an express mandate.

**Used in:** Reference for commune-submission counter-argument (architect acted as unauthorised "gestor") — potentially useful as background context in the small-claims case, but the more decisive article is 1375.

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. 1372. Lorsque volontairement, on gère l'affaire d'autrui, soit que le propriétaire connaisse la gestion, soit qu'il l'ignore, celui qui gère contracte l'engagement tacite de continuer la gestion qu'il a commencée, et de l'achever jusqu'à ce que le propriétaire soit en état d'y pourvoir lui-même; il doit se charger également de toutes les dépendances de cette même affaire. Il se soumet à toutes les obligations qui résulteraient d'un mandat exprès que lui aurait donné le propriétaire.

**English translation:**

> Art. 1372. Where a person voluntarily manages the affairs of another — whether the owner knows of the management or not — the manager tacitly undertakes to continue the management they have begun and to complete it until the owner is in a position to take it over personally; the manager must also attend to all accessory matters of the same affair. The manager is subject to all the obligations that would arise from an express mandate given by the owner.

### Article 1375 — Gestion d'affaires (reimbursement limits)

**Subject:** Reimbursement is owed **only if the affair was "bien administrée" (well managed)**. The principal must then honour engagements contracted in their name, indemnify the gestor for personal undertakings, and reimburse useful or necessary expenses.

**Used in:** `ORIGINAL-CONTRACT-RESPONSE.md` (as reference; any compensation for work under the original contract is conditional on the work having been well managed — our position: design errors, delays, regulatory misses, and an unmodifiable permit fail that test).

**Key phrase for our use:** *"bien administrée"* — this is the hinge. If the architect's management wasn't "well" done, reimbursement is not owed.

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. 1375. Le maître dont l'affaire a été bien administrée, doit remplir les engagements que le gérant a contractés en son nom, l'indemniser de tous les engagements personnels qu'il a pris et lui rembourser toutes les dépenses utiles ou nécessaires qu'il a faites.

**English translation:**

> Art. 1375. The principal whose affair has been well managed must honour the engagements the manager has contracted in the principal's name, indemnify the manager for all personal undertakings, and reimburse all useful or necessary expenses made.

### Article 1984 — Mandat (definition)

**Subject:** Defines the mandate: an act by which one person gives another the power to do something for and in the name of the principal; the contract is formed only by the mandatary's acceptance.

**Used in:** Background context for mandat-based arguments (Arts. 1989 and 1998 are the operative rules).

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. 1984. Le mandat ou procuration est un acte par lequel une personne donne à une autre le pouvoir de faire quelque chose pour le mandant et en son nom.
>
> Le contrat ne se forme que par l'acceptation du mandataire.

**English translation:**

> Art. 1984. The mandate, or power of attorney, is an act by which one person gives another the power to do something for the principal and in the principal's name.
>
> The contract is formed only by the mandatary's acceptance.

---

### Article 1989 — Mandat (limits of power)

**Subject:** The mandatary cannot do anything beyond what is set out in the mandate.

**Used in:** Supporting context for the "acts beyond mandate" argument (Article 1998 is the more direct rule).

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. 1989. Le mandataire ne peut rien faire au-delà de ce qui est porté dans son mandat: le pouvoir de transiger ne renferme pas celui de compromettre.

**English translation:**

> Art. 1989. The mandatary cannot do anything beyond what is set out in their mandate: the power to settle does not include the power to submit to arbitration.

### Article 1184 — Résolution of synallagmatic contracts for non-performance

**Subject:** In bilateral (synallagmatic) contracts, a resolutory condition is always implied for the case where one party fails to perform. The aggrieved party may either compel performance or seek judicial *résolution* (termination) of the contract with damages. Résolution has retroactive (ex tunc) effect — parties must be restored to their pre-contractual position, meaning payments received by the defaulting party must be returned.

**Used in:** `33K-DEMAND-Response-DRAFT.md` reservation-of-rights paragraph (basis for restitution of the €6,581.25 paid under the original contract). Primary legal basis for the `ORIGINAL-CONTRACT-RESPONSE.md` small-claims-court case.

**Key Luxembourg jurisprudence:** Luxembourg Cour de cassation decisions (e.g., 23 May 2019, 87/19; 27 April 2017, 39/17) routinely apply Article 1184 together with Article 1183 in fee-restitution disputes. The retroactive effect is expressed as: "la résolution produit l'anéantissement du contrat et la restitution des prestations."

**French text (verbatim, Napoleonic text retained in Luxembourg):**

> Art. 1184. La condition résolutoire est toujours sous-entendue dans les contrats synallagmatiques, pour le cas où l'une des deux parties ne satisfera point à son engagement.
>
> Dans ce cas, le contrat n'est point résolu de plein droit. La partie envers laquelle l'engagement n'a point été exécuté, a le choix ou de forcer l'autre à l'exécution de la convention lorsqu'elle est possible, ou d'en demander la résolution avec dommages et intérêts.
>
> La résolution doit être demandée en justice, et il peut être accordé au défendeur un délai selon les circonstances.

**English translation:**

> Art. 1184. A resolutory condition is always implied in synallagmatic contracts, for the case where one of the two parties does not fulfil its engagement.
>
> In such case, the contract is not automatically resolved. The party towards whom the engagement has not been performed has the choice either to compel the other party to perform the agreement where this is possible, or to seek its resolution together with damages.
>
> Resolution must be sought judicially, and the defendant may be granted a period of grace according to the circumstances.

### Article 1998 — Mandat (acts beyond mandate / ratification)

**Subject:** The principal is bound by engagements contracted within the granted power; the principal is bound by acts exceeding that power **only insofar as they have expressly or tacitly ratified them**.

**Used in:** `33K-DEMAND-Response-DRAFT.md` Section 4 (as the correct legal hook for unauthorised commune submission — replacing the earlier, wrong citation to Article 1156).

**Key phrase for our use:** *"Il n'est tenu de ce qui a pu être fait au-delà, qu'autant qu'il l'a ratifié expressément ou tacitement."* The architect acted without mandate; we never ratified; therefore the act doesn't bind us.

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. 1998. Le mandant est tenu d'exécuter les engagements contractés par le mandataire, conformément au pouvoir qui lui a été donné.
>
> Il n'est tenu de ce qui a pu être fait au-delà, qu'autant qu'il l'a ratifié expressément ou tacitement.

**English translation:**

> Art. 1998. The principal is required to perform the engagements contracted by the mandatary in accordance with the power given to them.
>
> The principal is bound by what may have been done beyond that power only insofar as they have ratified it expressly or tacitly.

## 2. Code de la consommation luxembourgeois

Source: [legilux.public.lu — Code de la consommation (consolidated)](https://legilux.public.lu/eli/etat/leg/code/consommation/20241231)

**Important note on numbering:** after the 2014 re-codification, the Code de la consommation goes L.121-1 → L.121-2 → **directly to L.122-1**. There are NO substantive articles L.121-3 through L.121-7. Old drafts or guides that cite L.121-3, L.121-6, or L.121-7 are using outdated numbering.

### Application to architects and other regulated professions (verified 2026-04-16)

**Yes, the Code de la consommation (Articles L.122-1 et seq.) applies to architect-to-private-client relationships in Luxembourg.** Key points verified via research:

1. **"Produit" expressly includes services and immovable property.** L.121-2(1): *"tout bien ou tout service, y compris les biens immeubles (…), ainsi que les droits et les obligations."*
2. **"Pratique commerciale" is broad.** L.121-2(2) covers any act, omission, conduct or commercial communication by a professional directly connected with the promotion, sale or supply of a product to consumers — which includes pitches, misrepresentations, billing practices and debt-collection tactics by an architect toward a private homeowner.
3. **"Diligence professionnelle" explicitly includes liberal professions.** L.121-2(6): *"usages honnêtes en matière commerciale, industrielle, artisanale **ou libérale**."* Architects are a profession libérale.
4. **The "regulated profession" carve-out at L.121-1(2)(4°) is NOT an exclusion.** It applies *"sans préjudice"* (without prejudice to) deontology codes — meaning the consumer code and the 1992 OAI deontology regulation apply **in parallel**. One does not displace the other.
5. **CJEU case law** has applied the underlying UCPD (Directive 2005/29/EC) to regulated professions including lawyers. Member states cannot exempt regulated professions wholesale.
6. **Luxembourg government guidance** (guichet.public.lu on *pratiques commerciales déloyales*) confirms the regime applies to *"tous les professionnels en matière commerciale, industrielle, artisanale ou libérale."*

### Practical scope considerations

The Code applies with full force to **commercial** conduct (marketing pitches, misrepresentations that induce purchase decisions, billing and collection practices, aggressive ultimatums). It applies less directly to pure **technical-quality** disputes over professional deliverables — those are governed primarily by contract law and OAI deontology. For architect disputes, this means:

| Conduct | Primary regime | Code de la consommation applies? |
|---|---|---|
| Misleading statements about feasibility/timelines/permit modifiability that induce decisions | Consumer + deontology | **Yes — L.122-2 / L.122-3** |
| Demanding payment for unrequested services | Consumer (per se aggressive) | **Yes — L.122-7(6)** directly |
| 9-day ultimatum with legal threats | Consumer | **Yes — L.122-5, L.122-6** |
| Technical quality of design, execution of a survey | Contract + deontology | Only indirectly, via diligence professionnelle (L.122-1) |
| Breach of the written-convention requirement (Art. 8 RGD 1992) | OAI deontology | Not directly — this is a deontology violation |

### Article L.121-1 — Scope (note: not a substantive rule)

**Subject:** Defines the scope of the Title on unfair commercial practices — it applies to unfair B2C practices harming consumers' economic interests. Contains several carve-outs, including **(2)(4°) — codes of deontology for regulated professions** (opposing counsel could argue this partially displaces consumer law for architect conduct; countered by the fact that billing/demand practices are commercial, not professional).

**Used in:** Not directly cited in current drafts (the substantive rule is L.122-1; L.121-1 is only scope).

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. L. 121-1. (1) Le présent titre s'applique aux pratiques commerciales déloyales des professionnels vis-à-vis des consommateurs portant atteinte à leurs intérêts économiques et ce avant, pendant et après l'offre en vente et la vente de produits.
>
> (2) Le présent titre s'applique sans préjudice:
> 1) du droit des contrats, en particulier des règles relatives à la validité, à la formation ou aux effets des contrats;
> 2) des dispositions communautaires ou nationales relatives à la santé et à la sécurité des produits;
> 3) des dispositions législatives, réglementaires et administratives en matière de certification et d'indication du titre des ouvrages en métal précieux;
> 4) des conditions d'établissement ou des régimes d'autorisation ou des codes de déontologie ou de toute autre disposition spécifique régissant les professions réglementées;
> (…)
> 5) de l'article 3 de la loi modifiée du 30 juillet 2002 réglementant certaines pratiques commerciales, sanctionnant la concurrence déloyale (…).

**English translation:**

> Art. L. 121-1. (1) This Title applies to unfair commercial practices of professionals vis-à-vis consumers that harm their economic interests, before, during and after the offer for sale and the sale of products.
>
> (2) This Title applies without prejudice to:
> 1) contract law, in particular the rules relating to the validity, formation or effects of contracts;
> 2) Community or national provisions relating to the health and safety of products;
> 3) legislative, regulatory and administrative provisions on certification and the indication of hallmarks on precious-metal articles;
> 4) conditions of establishment, authorisation regimes, codes of deontology, or any other specific provision governing regulated professions;
> (…)
> 5) Article 3 of the amended law of 30 July 2002 regulating certain commercial practices, sanctioning unfair competition (…).

### Article L.121-2 — Definitions (including "diligence professionnelle")

**Subject:** Definitions section for the Title on unfair commercial practices. Defines: "produit," "pratique commerciale," "altération substantielle," "code de conduite," "diligence professionnelle," "invitation à l'achat," "influence injustifiée," "décision commerciale," "profession réglementée," "classement."

**Used in:** Not directly cited in current drafts (it is only a definitional article; the operative rule is L.122-1). Previously miscited as a prohibition — corrected to L.122-2/L.122-3 (for misleading practices) and L.122-1 (for the diligence rule).

> 6) «diligence professionnelle»: le niveau de compétence spécialisée et de soins dont le professionnel est raisonnablement censé faire preuve vis-à-vis du consommateur, conformément aux usages honnêtes en matière commerciale, industrielle, artisanale ou libérale dans son domaine d'activité;

### Article L.122-1 — General prohibition of unfair practices

**Subject:** The foundational prohibition: unfair commercial practices are forbidden, defined by a two-pronged test (contrary to professional diligence + materially distorts the consumer's economic behaviour).

**Used in:** `33K-DEMAND-Response-DRAFT.md` Section 4 bullet on "manquement à la diligence professionnelle" (replacing the earlier, weak citation to L.121-1).

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. L. 122-1. (1) Les pratiques commerciales déloyales sont interdites.
>
> (2) Une pratique commerciale est déloyale si:
> a) elle est contraire aux exigences de la diligence professionnelle et
> b) elle altère ou est susceptible d'altérer de manière substantielle le comportement économique, par rapport au produit, du consommateur moyen qu'elle touche ou auquel elle s'adresse, ou du membre moyen du groupe lorsqu'une pratique commerciale est ciblée vers un groupe particulier de consommateurs.
>
> (3) Les pratiques commerciales qui sont susceptibles d'altérer de manière substantielle le comportement économique d'un groupe clairement identifiable de consommateurs parce que ceux-ci sont particulièrement vulnérables (…) sont évaluées du point de vue du membre moyen de ce groupe. (…)
>
> (4) En particulier, sont déloyales les pratiques commerciales qui sont trompeuses, au sens de la section 1, ou agressives, au sens de la section 2.

**English translation:**

> Art. L. 122-1. (1) Unfair commercial practices are prohibited.
>
> (2) A commercial practice is unfair if:
> a) it is contrary to the requirements of professional diligence, and
> b) it materially distorts or is likely to materially distort the economic behaviour, with regard to the product, of the average consumer whom it reaches or to whom it is addressed, or of the average member of the group where a commercial practice is targeted at a particular group of consumers.
>
> (3) Commercial practices which are likely to materially distort the economic behaviour of a clearly identifiable group of consumers who are particularly vulnerable (…) shall be assessed from the perspective of the average member of that group. (…)
>
> (4) In particular, commercial practices which are misleading within the meaning of Section 1, or aggressive within the meaning of Section 2, are unfair.

### Article L.122-2 — Misleading commercial practices (actions)

**Subject:** Defines misleading commercial actions — false information, or deceptive presentation (even if factually correct) about product existence/nature, characteristics, price, scope of the professional's commitments, the consumer's rights, etc.

**Used in:** `33K-DEMAND-Response-DRAFT.md` Section 4 bullet on "pratiques commerciales trompeuses" (replacing the earlier miscitation to L.121-2). Should also replace L.121-2 in `ORIGINAL-CONTRACT-RESPONSE.md` and `LEGAL-LANGUAGE-REFERENCE.md`.

**French text (verbatim, core paragraph):**

> Art. L. 122-2. (1) Une pratique commerciale est réputée trompeuse:
> 1) si elle contient des informations fausses; ou
> 2) si, d'une manière quelconque, y compris par sa présentation générale, elle induit ou est susceptible d'induire en erreur le consommateur moyen, même si les informations présentées sont factuellement correctes, en ce qui concerne un ou plusieurs des éléments ci-après et que, dans un cas comme dans l'autre, elle l'amène ou est susceptible de l'amener à prendre une décision commerciale qu'il n'aurait pas prise autrement. Ces éléments concernent:
> a) l'existence ou la nature du produit;
> b) les caractéristiques principales du produit (…);
> c) l'étendue des engagements du professionnel (…);
> d) le prix ou le mode de calcul du prix (…);
> e) la nécessité d'un service (…);
> f) la nature, les qualités et les droits du professionnel (…);
> g) les droits du consommateur (…).

**English translation (core paragraph):**

> Art. L. 122-2. (1) A commercial practice is regarded as misleading:
> 1) if it contains false information; or
> 2) if, in any way, including by its overall presentation, it deceives or is likely to deceive the average consumer, even if the information is factually correct, in relation to one or more of the elements listed below, and in either case causes or is likely to cause the consumer to take a commercial decision that they would not have taken otherwise. These elements concern:
> a) the existence or nature of the product;
> b) the main characteristics of the product (…);
> c) the extent of the professional's commitments (…);
> d) the price or the manner in which the price is calculated (…);
> e) the need for a service (…);
> f) the nature, attributes and rights of the professional (…);
> g) the rights of the consumer (…).

### Article L.122-3 — Misleading commercial practices (omissions)

**Subject:** Defines misleading omissions — failure to provide material information the average consumer needs for an informed decision, or hiding/obscuring it, causing a different commercial decision.

**Used in:** `33K-DEMAND-Response-DRAFT.md` Section 4 bullet on "pratiques commerciales trompeuses" (alongside L.122-2). Should also replace L.121-3 in `ORIGINAL-CONTRACT-RESPONSE.md` and `LEGAL-LANGUAGE-REFERENCE.md`.

**Why this fits our case:** the architect omitted the construction-cost estimate he himself said was the *"important basis"* for decision-making. Classic material omission.

**French text (verbatim, core paragraphs):**

> Art. L. 122-3. (1) Une pratique commerciale est considérée comme une omission trompeuse si, dans son contexte factuel, compte tenu de toutes ses caractéristiques et des circonstances ainsi que des limites propres au moyen de communication utilisé, elle omet une information substantielle dont le consommateur moyen a besoin, compte tenu du contexte, pour prendre une décision commerciale en connaissance de cause et, par conséquent, l'amène ou est susceptible de l'amener à prendre une décision commerciale qu'il n'aurait pas prise autrement.
>
> (2) Est également considérée comme une omission trompeuse, une pratique commerciale par laquelle un professionnel (…) dissimule une information substantielle (…) ou la fournit de façon peu claire, inintelligible, ambiguë ou à contretemps (…) et lorsque (…) le consommateur moyen est ainsi amené ou est susceptible d'être amené à prendre une décision commerciale qu'il n'aurait pas prise autrement.

**English translation (core paragraphs):**

> Art. L. 122-3. (1) A commercial practice is regarded as a misleading omission if, in its factual context, taking account of all its features and circumstances and the limitations of the medium of communication used, it omits material information that the average consumer needs, according to the context, in order to take an informed commercial decision and thereby causes or is likely to cause the average consumer to take a commercial decision that they would not have taken otherwise.
>
> (2) A commercial practice is also regarded as a misleading omission where a professional (…) conceals material information (…) or provides it in an unclear, unintelligible, ambiguous or untimely manner, (…) and where (…) this causes or is likely to cause the average consumer to take a commercial decision that they would not have taken otherwise.

### Article L.122-5 — Aggressive commercial practices (general)

**Subject:** Defines aggressive commercial practice — one that significantly impairs the average consumer's freedom of choice or conduct through harassment, coercion, or undue influence, causing a commercial decision they would not otherwise have taken.

**Used in:** `33K-DEMAND-Response-DRAFT.md` Section 4, bullet on "pratiques commerciales agressives" AND in the paragraph about the 9-day deadline (replacing the earlier, non-existent L.121-6/L.121-7).

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. L. 122-5. Une pratique commerciale est réputée agressive si, dans son contexte factuel, compte tenu de toutes ses caractéristiques et des circonstances, elle altère ou est susceptible d'altérer de manière significative, du fait du harcèlement, de la contrainte, y compris le recours à la force physique, ou d'une influence injustifiée, la liberté de choix ou de conduite du consommateur moyen à l'égard d'un produit, et, par conséquent, l'amène ou est susceptible de l'amener à prendre une décision commerciale qu'il n'aurait pas prise autrement.

**English translation:**

> Art. L. 122-5. A commercial practice is regarded as aggressive if, in its factual context, taking account of all its features and circumstances, it significantly impairs or is likely to significantly impair — through harassment, coercion (including the use of physical force) or undue influence — the average consumer's freedom of choice or conduct with regard to a product, thereby causing or being likely to cause them to take a commercial decision that they would not have taken otherwise.

### Article L.122-6 — Aggressive commercial practices (factors)

**Subject:** Lists the factors to determine whether a practice amounts to harassment, coercion or undue influence.

**Used in:** `33K-DEMAND-Response-DRAFT.md` Section 4.

**Most relevant items for our case:**
- **(d)** disproportionate non-contractual obstacles imposed when the consumer wishes to exercise contractual rights (fits the architect's block on the client terminating/switching)
- **(e)** threats of action that cannot legally be taken (relevant to the 9-day ultimatum + threat of recouvrement proceedings for unauthorised charges)

**French text (verbatim):**

> Art. L. 122-6. Afin de déterminer si une pratique commerciale recourt au harcèlement, à la contrainte, y compris la force physique, ou à une influence injustifiée, les éléments suivants sont pris en considération:
> a) le moment et l'endroit où la pratique est mise en œuvre, sa nature et sa persistance;
> b) le recours à la menace physique ou verbale;
> c) l'exploitation en connaissance de cause par le professionnel de tout malheur ou circonstance particulière d'une gravité propre à altérer le jugement du consommateur, dans le but d'influencer la décision du consommateur à l'égard du produit;
> d) tout obstacle non contractuel important ou disproportionné imposé par le professionnel lorsque le consommateur souhaite faire valoir ses droits contractuels, et notamment celui de mettre fin au contrat ou de changer de produit ou de fournisseur;
> e) toute menace d'action alors que cette action n'est pas légalement possible.

**English translation:**

> Art. L. 122-6. In order to determine whether a commercial practice uses harassment, coercion (including physical force) or undue influence, the following shall be taken into account:
> a) the time, location, nature and persistence of the practice;
> b) the use of physical or verbal threats;
> c) the professional's knowing exploitation of any misfortune or specific circumstance so serious as to impair the consumer's judgement, with a view to influencing the consumer's decision in relation to the product;
> d) any onerous or disproportionate non-contractual obstacle imposed by the professional where the consumer wishes to exercise their contractual rights, including the right to terminate a contract or to switch to another product or professional;
> e) any threat to take action that cannot legally be taken.

## 3bis. Luxembourg doctrinal concepts on architect fee disputes

These are not articles but **jurisprudential / doctrinal principles** that complement the Code civil articles above. They are especially relevant to the `ORIGINAL-CONTRACT-RESPONSE.md` small-claims-court case.

### Exception d'inexécution (*exceptio non adimpleti contractus*)

**What it is:** A defensive remedy. In a synallagmatic contract, one party may refuse to perform its own obligation while the other party has not performed theirs. The party invoking the exception is not in default so long as the other party's non-performance persists.

**Source:** No single codified article in Luxembourg — derives from the synallagmatic nature of contracts (Arts. 1102–1104 Code civil) and consistent Luxembourg jurisprudence. Unlike France post-2016 (Arts. 1219–1220), Luxembourg applies it as a general principle.

**Conditions (jurisprudential):**
1. The contract is synallagmatic (both parties have obligations).
2. The other party's non-performance is sufficiently serious.
3. Good faith invocation (not opportunistic).

**Used in:** Supports our **refusal to pay the €33,930** in the demand response. Also supports the position in the `ORIGINAL-CONTRACT-RESPONSE.md` case that we were entitled to suspend further payment given the architect's non-performance.

---

### Mission non accomplie

**What it is:** A doctrine specific to architect fee disputes. Architect contracts typically phase fees against defined stages (avant-projet, projet d'exécution, suivi de chantier, etc.). If the architect has not completed a phase but bills for it, the fees for that phase must be reduced or refused. Luxembourg courts (and appointed experts) will assess the "coût réel" of the work actually completed to determine an equitable fee.

**Source:** Luxembourg jurisprudence on architect fee disputes; OAI's standard architect contract (*contrat-type*) provides in Article 24 that on termination, the architect is entitled to fees for services actually provided plus 30% of fees for services not yet provided.

**Used in:** Primary doctrinal framing for the `ORIGINAL-CONTRACT-RESPONSE.md` case — the €6,581.25 corresponded to a full mission that was never accomplished (design errors, regulatory misses, unmodifiable permit, no feasible timeline → no usable deliverable).

---

### Prestations inexploitables (unusable deliverables)

**What it is:** A specific evidentiary line of jurisprudence — where the client can prove that the architect's deliverables are genuinely unusable for their intended purpose, the client may refuse payment of the corresponding fees (or seek restitution of fees already paid). The burden of proof lies on the client.

**Source:** Consistent jurisprudence (see e.g. French Cour de cassation, 3e ch. civ., 12 novembre 2020, n° 842 — the principle is applied similarly in Luxembourg). In Luxembourg, the burden-of-proof rule is Art. 1315 Code civil (*"celui qui se prétend libéré, doit justifier le paiement ou le fait qui a produit l'extinction de son obligation"*).

**Conditions:**
1. Client must prove the deliverable is *genuinely unusable* (not merely inconvenient or suboptimal).
2. Unusability must be attributable to the architect's fault (design errors, regulatory non-compliance, incorrect professional advice).

**Used in:** Central evidentiary framing for the `ORIGINAL-CONTRACT-RESPONSE.md` case — the unmodifiable permit, the regulatory misses, and the timeline-feasibility failures combine to make the delivered permit unusable for our intended purpose.
